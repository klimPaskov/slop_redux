## Research classification

Every later specification claim must use one of these labels.

| Label | Meaning | Use in design |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `H` | Documented history | Real institutions, events, people, policies, disputes, movements, material limits, and recorded proposals |
| `P` | Plausible speculation | A divergence that grows from real actors or pressures but did not occur in the documented form used by the design |
| `A` | Deliberate absurdity | A culturally grounded impossible, supernatural, anachronistic, or intentionally extreme outcome |

The labels describe historical status, not gameplay value. A documented route can still be difficult and an absurd route can still require coherent strategy.

## Source hierarchy

The research uses sources in this order of preference:

1. Irish government records, statutes, constitutional texts, military archives, census material, and diplomatic documents.
2. Archival institutions and academic publications.
3. The Dictionary of Irish Biography and university-supported historical work.
4. Reputable historical magazines and public history institutions.
5. Secondary summaries only as leads when stronger sources are not immediately accessible.

When sources disagree, the design uses the more authoritative or more narrowly evidenced claim and records the disagreement.

## Historical design thesis

Ireland in 1936 is a young state whose institutions have survived a revolutionary split without resolving the meaning of independence. The government can legislate, hold elections, control most of the island, and conduct foreign policy. It still faces a constitutional transition, the memory of civil war, an armed republican movement, economic dependence, religious influence, weak defence capacity, and the unresolved status of Northern Ireland.

The Second World War does not erase those problems. It compresses them. Neutrality becomes a test of sovereignty, military weakness, trade access, political unity, intelligence policy, and relations with Britain. The return of the Treaty Ports in 1938 gives the state greater control and a heavier burden. Wartime shortages force stronger economic administration. Covert cooperation with the Allies coexists with public neutrality. Republican and fascist subversion create security pressures. Northern Ireland becomes more industrially valuable, more militarised, and more exposed to bombing.

The overhaul should therefore make the player govern several competing definitions of the republic at once:

- a constitutional state founded through revolution
- an incomplete national project because of partition
- a Catholic-majority society with democratic institutions and powerful clerical influence
- a rural economy seeking industrial capacity
- a formally neutral state with security ties and dependencies
- a country whose army can mobilise people faster than it can equip them
- a cultural nation that promotes Irish language and identity while functioning largely through English-language administration and external trade

## Chronological research frame

### 1921 to 1923: Treaty, partition, and civil war

`H` The Anglo-Irish Treaty created the Irish Free State as a dominion and allowed Northern Ireland to opt out. The treaty split the revolutionary movement and led to civil war between pro-Treaty and anti-Treaty forces.

`H` The Civil War created durable networks of loyalty, memory, imprisonment, execution, and mistrust. These loyalties later shaped party identity, security policy, commemorations, and attitudes toward the IRA.

Design consequence:

- The Civil War cannot be reduced to a static national spirit.
- Former comrades, military institutions, parties, families, and local organisations must affect the internal struggle.
- A route that reconciles the split should play differently from one that criminalises, exploits, or reopens it.

### 1926 to 1932: Fianna Fáil enters constitutional politics

`H` Fianna Fáil was founded by Éamon de Valera after the anti-Treaty movement divided over abstention from the Free State parliament. Its entry into Dáil politics converted much of anti-Treaty republicanism into constitutional opposition.

`H` Cumann na nGaedheal remained the governing pro-Treaty party until 1932 and built many of the state's administrative and legal institutions.

Design consequence:

- Fianna Fáil's legitimacy rests on combining republican revision with control of the existing state.
- Fine Gael's legitimacy rests on defending constitutional continuity, public order, and institutional survival.
- The two main parties are rivals within the state, not generic ideological opposites imported from continental Europe.

### 1932 to 1938: Fianna Fáil government and the Economic War

`H` Fianna Fáil entered government in 1932. Its government dismantled remaining treaty-era constraints, pursued protection and industrial policy, and withheld land annuity payments to Britain.

`H` Britain and Ireland imposed retaliatory trade restrictions in the conflict commonly called the Economic War. Agriculture and export sectors bore heavy pressure.

`H` Seán Lemass was central to industrial and commercial policy. The Control of Manufactures framework and protective policies sought Irish ownership and domestic industry, though the economy remained small and heavily rural.

`H` The 1938 British-Irish agreements settled financial and trade disputes and transferred British-controlled defence facilities at Berehaven, Cobh, and Lough Swilly to Ireland.

Design consequence:

- The player should inherit unfinished protectionism rather than a blank economy.
- Ending, extending, or replacing the Economic War should change agriculture, imports, industrial ownership, relations with Britain, and defence timing.
- The Treaty Ports should be a strategic handover that creates infrastructure, garrison, naval, and diplomatic obligations.

### 1933 to 1936: Fine Gael, the Blueshirts, and a declining fascist opportunity

`H` Fine Gael was created from Cumann na nGaedheal, the National Centre Party, and the Army Comrades Association associated with the Blueshirts.

`H` Eoin O'Duffy became the first Fine Gael president, then broke with the party. His later National Corporate Party and Spanish Civil War brigade represented a smaller and more openly authoritarian movement.

`H` By the 1936 start, O'Duffy's mass political opportunity had already narrowed. A successful corporatist takeover would require a new crisis, state failure, foreign prestige, or a coalition with other discontented groups.

Design consequence:

- A corporatist route can exist from game start only as a weak pressure network or crisis opportunity.
- Fine Gael should not be treated as an automatic fascist party.
- O'Duffy's route requires deliberate destabilisation, paramilitary organisation, and a break with constitutional opposition.

### 1934 to 1937: Republican Congress and the fractured left

`H` The Republican Congress attempted to connect republicanism with class politics, anti-fascism, labour organisation, and a thirty-two-county social programme.

`H` Peadar O'Donnell and Frank Ryan were major figures associated with this current. The movement fractured and did not become a mass electoral party.

`H` Irish volunteers fought on opposing sides in Spain. O'Duffy led a brigade on the Nationalist side, while Frank Ryan and other Irish left-wing volunteers fought with the International Brigades for the Spanish Republic.

Design consequence:

- Radical left content should emerge through cooperation among labour militants, socialist republicans, anti-fascist veterans, and local organisations.
- A workers' republic cannot be an easy Labour Party ideology switch.
- Spain provides experience, prestige, trauma, factional rivalry, and foreign contacts for both radical right and radical left routes.

### 1937: Constitution and state identity

`H` The Constitution was enacted by referendum on 1 July 1937 and came into operation on 29 December 1937.

`H` It named the state Éire or Ireland, declared it sovereign, independent, and democratic, established a directly elected president, made Irish the first official language, and retained parliamentary government.

`H` Article 28 required Dáil assent for participation in war. The First Amendment in 1939 extended emergency provisions to conflicts in which Ireland was not a participant.

`H` The original religious article recognised the special position of the Catholic Church and also recognised named minority denominations. It did not make Catholicism the state religion.

`H` John Charles McQuaid influenced parts of the constitutional drafting, especially social and family questions, but the constitution was not simply his document and it did not adopt the full authoritarian Catholic programme advanced by some contemporary movements.

`H` The original Articles 2 and 3 expressed a constitutional claim concerning the whole island while limiting the immediate exercise of law to the territory governed by the state.

Design consequence:

- Constitution-making is a playable settlement rather than a free stability focus.
- The player should balance executive authority, parliamentary legitimacy, language policy, religious influence, minority guarantees, and the territorial claim.
- Emergency constitutional powers should later interact with censorship, detention, rationing, security, and war participation.

### 1938: Return of the Treaty Ports

`H` The 1938 agreement transferred British Admiralty property, harbour defences, fixed armaments, and associated facilities at Berehaven, Cobh, and Lough Swilly.

`H` The transfer strengthened Irish control over national territory and made wartime neutrality more credible.

`H` It also left Ireland responsible for coastal facilities and waters that it lacked the fleet, aircraft, anti-aircraft weapons, communications, and trained personnel to protect fully.

Design consequence:

- The handover should open a choice between rapid Irish fortification, limited cooperation, dispersed coastwatching, and acceptance of risk.
- Failure to prepare the ports should affect invasion planning, smuggling, intelligence penetration, neutrality violations, and foreign pressure.

### 1939: Neutrality, emergency law, IRA campaign, and censorship

`H` Joseph Walshe described Ireland's position in August 1939 as that of a neutral state, while acknowledging that the state's recent history and continuing ties to Britain made its neutrality unlike that of older neutral states.

`H` De Valera's government declared neutrality at the outbreak of war. Mainstream political support for neutrality was broad.

`H` The Emergency Powers Act and the constitutional amendment enabled extensive controls, including censorship, economic direction, detention, and emergency administration.

`H` The IRA's S-Plan bombing campaign in Britain and its internal activity increased security pressure. The Irish state used detention, military courts, executions, hunger-strike confrontation, and organisational suppression against the IRA during the Emergency.

Design consequence:

- Neutrality and domestic repression must interact.
- Republican mobilisation can grow when the state appears to compromise sovereignty, but indiscriminate IRA violence should reduce social consent and invite stronger repression.
- Censorship should protect neutrality and public order while generating legitimacy costs, hidden information, and route-specific abuses.

### 1939 to 1942: Defence expansion

`H` The Defence Forces created emergency plans for invasion and wartime policy.

`H` The Marine and Coast Watching Service was established in 1939. Military Archives records 83 lookout posts manned by the Local Defence Force, tasked with reporting shipping, aircraft, and shore communications.

`H` One public-history account refers to 88 concrete posts. The design should use the official Military Archives figure of 83 for historical presentation and treat any implementation count as a map abstraction.

`H` The regular army expanded to nearly 40,000 by early 1942 and formed two divisions, but equipment shortages remained serious.

`H` The Local Security Force and later Local Defence Force mobilised a much larger volunteer base for local defence.

`H` A very small marine force carried out patrol and mine-related duties with limited vessels and personnel.

Design consequence:

- Ireland can raise manpower quickly only at the cost of equipment, training, supply, work-force availability, and command quality.
- Coastal defence should be a network problem that combines observation, communications, local units, naval patrol, air defence, and port control.
- A militarised route can improve readiness faster but should increase fiscal burden and internal political pressure.

### 1940: Fall of France, invasion danger, unity proposal, and secret liaison

`H` The collapse of France sharply increased fears of German or British intervention in Ireland.

`H` British proposals in June 1940 linked Irish entry into the war and defence cooperation with movement toward Irish unity. De Valera rejected a bargain that did not provide immediate unity and emphasised Ireland's lack of war material.

`H` British observers recorded unusually strong national unity around neutrality and resistance to any belligerent that violated it.

`H` The 18th Military Mission involved Irish officers and British and American officials discussing preparations for an Axis invasion.

Design consequence:

- June 1940 is a major crisis window with several possible outcomes, not a single historical event button.
- A wartime unity bargain must require Northern Irish, British, Irish, and military conditions. It cannot be accepted as a simple territorial transfer.
- Secret liaison can improve defence while increasing exposure, republican suspicion, and German pressure.

### 1941: Shipping crisis, Belfast Blitz, and Northern conscription pressure

`H` Irish Shipping was established during the Emergency to secure essential supplies and operate a strategic merchant fleet under dangerous conditions.

`H` Northern Ireland's industrial role grew through shipbuilding, engineering, aircraft, munitions, textiles, and associated labour mobilisation.

`H` Belfast suffered four major Luftwaffe attacks in April and May 1941. One detailed account gives about 1,100 deaths, more than 56,000 damaged houses, and roughly 100,000 people temporarily homeless.

`H` The prospect of conscription in Northern Ireland generated political conflict. It affected unionist government, nationalist opposition, church positions, labour mobilisation, and relations with Dublin.

Design consequence:

- The Belfast Blitz must create cross-border humanitarian, intelligence, air-defence, and political choices.
- Shipping is a strategic system tied to ports, convoys, foreign charters, crews, losses, imports, and rationing.
- Northern conscription can create several coalitions, including nationalist opposition, labour opposition, southern diplomatic intervention, and unionist demands for equal wartime burden.

### 1942: Peak mobilisation and Ailtirí emergence

`H` Irish military and local defence mobilisation reached high wartime levels around 1942.

`H` Ailtirí na hAiséirghe emerged publicly during the Emergency under Gearóid Ó Cuinneagáin. Its network grew through Irish-language and Gaelic revival circles and promoted an authoritarian, fascist, anti-democratic, and aggressively Gaelic programme.

`H` The movement's public emergence belongs to the wartime context. It should not be placed on the same starting footing as Fianna Fáil, Fine Gael, or Labour in 1936.

Design consequence:

- Ailtirí requires hidden preconditions such as emergency authoritarianism, language radicalisation, failures of the established parties, fascist foreign success, and an organised cultural network.
- The route must not romanticise its fascism or antisemitism.
- Its cultural material can overlap with legitimate Gaelic revival institutions, but the design must distinguish coercive totalitarian appropriation from ordinary language and cultural policy.

### 1944: Weather intelligence and Allied operational dependence

`H` Irish weather reporting from Blacksod Point contributed to the Allied decision to delay the Normandy landings from 5 June to 6 June 1944.

`H` This cooperation demonstrates how neutral technical services could become strategically important without public entry into the war.

Design consequence:

- Technical cooperation, meteorology, navigation, rescue, and intelligence can provide meaningful neutrality gameplay.
- The player should face choices about secrecy, reciprocal equipment, diplomatic deniability, and domestic disclosure.

### 1945: End of the Emergency and unresolved postwar questions

`H` Ireland emerged from the European war without formal belligerence, with stronger claims to independent foreign policy and with severe economic strains.

`H` Wartime institutions, emergency powers, censorship practices, military mobilisation, shipping experience, industrial administration, and cross-border relations created postwar choices rather than disappearing immediately.

Design consequence:

- Every route needs a postwar settlement.
- Demobilisation, veterans, emergency laws, trade, housing, industrial policy, Northern Ireland, international recognition, and constitutional identity must determine the late-game conclusion.

## Starting political system in 1936

### Fianna Fáil

Classification: `H`

Core position:

- governing republican constitutional party
- led by Éamon de Valera
- committed to revising treaty-era arrangements through state power
- socially broad, with rural, urban, republican, and administrative currents
- associated with protectionism and state-led industrial initiatives

Internal design currents:

1. De Valera constitutional republicanism
2. Seán Lemass developmental modernism
3. security and emergency government associated with figures such as Oscar Traynor
4. hard republican expectations among supporters who still see partition and treaty legacies as unfinished

Historical constraint:

Fianna Fáil can become more presidential, more developmental, more conciliatory, or more coercive. It should not begin as a generic authoritarian party.

### Fine Gael

Classification: `H`

Core position:

- constitutional opposition rooted in the pro-Treaty state-building tradition
- led by W. T. Cosgrave at the 1936 start
- associated with legality, fiscal caution, agriculture, Commonwealth experience, and public order
- burdened by the recent Blueshirt association and O'Duffy split

Internal design currents:

1. Cosgrave institutional conservatism
2. Richard Mulcahy's military and organisational authority
3. John A. Costello's legal and constitutional approach
4. James Dillon's outspoken pro-Allied position
5. agrarian and business interests that can resist both prolonged protectionism and radical redistribution

Historical constraint:

Fine Gael should be able to reconcile the state, strengthen security, reopen Commonwealth cooperation, or support Allied entry. A fascist outcome requires a separate break led by marginal corporatists.

### Labour Party and trade union movement

Classification: `H`

Core position:

- parliamentary labour party led by William Norton
- connected to organised labour and social reform
- broadly supportive of neutrality during the Emergency
- weaker than the two Civil War parties but capable of coalition leverage

Internal design currents:

1. parliamentary social democracy
2. trade union bargaining and emergency wage policy
3. anti-conscription and anti-fascist activism
4. rural labour and small-farmer concerns
5. possible cooperation with socialist republican veterans

Historical constraint:

Labour cannot be treated as automatically pro-British or automatically revolutionary. Its radical route requires visible alliance-building, social crisis, and pressure from extra-parliamentary actors.

### IRA and militant republicanism

Classification: `H`

Core position:

- claims continuity with the revolutionary republic
- rejects or conditionally recognises the southern state
- considers partition and British presence unresolved
- divided over strategy, politics, social programme, and foreign contacts

Internal design currents:

1. orthodox Army Council militarism
2. border campaign priorities
3. German contact or opportunist anti-British diplomacy
4. socialist republican opposition
5. local networks that may prefer political agitation, arms preservation, or community defence over indiscriminate bombing

Historical constraint:

The IRA is dangerous but organisationally vulnerable. Its takeover requires severe state crisis, military defection, mass mobilisation, or foreign intervention. It cannot win a normal election by default.

### Republican Congress legacy and socialist republicanism

Classification: `H` for the actors and ideas, `P` for a durable unified route

Core position:

- thirty-two-county republicanism linked to class politics
- anti-fascism and support for the Spanish Republic
- opposition to both conservative state order and narrow military conspiracism
- interest in labour, tenant, small-farmer, and unemployed organisation

Historical constraint:

The real movement had fragmented. A successful route must rebuild a coalition among surviving activists, labour militants, veterans, and local republican groups.

### Catholic hierarchy and Catholic social influence

Classification: `H`

Core position:

- strong influence over education, health, social policy, public morality, and political legitimacy
- no single unified party command
- internal differences over constitutional wording, social reform, fascism, neutrality, labour, and relations with Britain

Design-relevant actors and institutions:

- bishops and archbishops
- parish networks
- religious orders
- Catholic social organisations
- educational and charitable institutions
- clerical advisers such as John Charles McQuaid

Historical constraint:

The Church is a power centre, not one mind. A vocational or clerically dominated state must result from choices that trade pluralism, labour autonomy, and parliamentary authority for social organisation and clerical sanction.

### Eoin O'Duffy and the National Corporate Party

Classification: `H` for the movement, `P` for a successful takeover after 1936

Core position:

- anti-communist, authoritarian, corporatist, and paramilitary politics
- personal prestige from revolutionary and policing service
- association with the Blueshirts and the Irish Brigade in Spain
- reduced mass appeal by the game start

Historical constraint:

The route requires a crisis coalition. It should compete for former Blueshirts, conservative Catholics, anti-communists, veterans, and security hardliners while facing opposition from Fine Gael constitutionalists and the state.

### Ailtirí na hAiséirghe

Classification: `H` after wartime emergence, `P` for state takeover

Core position:

- fascist and anti-democratic movement
- radical Irish-language programme
- attempted capture of respectable cultural institutions
- pan-Celtic and authoritarian ambitions
- strong dependence on wartime context and the perceived failure of existing parties

Historical constraint:

The route is hidden before its organisational conditions exist. It must not appear as a normal 1936 choice.

## Civil War legacy as active gameplay

The Civil War legacy should have at least six live components.

### Institutional memory

`H` The army, Garda, civil service, courts, and main parties retain different memories of legitimacy and betrayal.

Gameplay direction:

- appointments and reforms alter institutional loyalty
- purges improve short-term obedience and damage competence or consent
- reconciliation improves consent but may empower former opponents

### Commemoration and martyrdom

`H` Executions, prison experiences, hunger strikes, graves, anniversaries, and local memories remained politically potent.

Gameplay direction:

- commemorations can reconcile, mobilise, or inflame
- security deaths and executions can create new martyrs
- route events should use regional and family memory rather than generic national anger

### Arms and clandestine networks

`H` Militant republican networks retained weapons, contacts, and local knowledge.

Gameplay direction:

- amnesties can exchange arms for legitimacy
- raids can seize equipment but create resistance
- foreign war increases the value of clandestine networks to both the state and the IRA

### Party legitimacy

`H` Fianna Fáil and Fine Gael both claim to embody the legitimate national tradition through different readings of treaty and state survival.

Gameplay direction:

- constitutional changes shift legitimacy
- entry into war, British cooperation, repression, and unity policy reopen the old split

### Military obedience

`P` A severe crisis could revive uncertainty among officers with different revolutionary backgrounds.

Gameplay direction:

- military loyalty should be a threshold issue in coups, emergency government, or republican insurrection
- ordinary political competition should not constantly threaten civil war without cause

### Partition grievance

`H` Partition preserves the strongest unresolved revolutionary grievance.

Gameplay direction:

- progress on unity can strengthen state legitimacy
- perceived abandonment of the North can strengthen militant legitimacy
- reckless violence can reduce support among northern nationalists and southern voters

## Economy and social structure

### Rural weight

`H` The 1936 census records a society in which agricultural occupations remained central and county differences were substantial.

Design direction:

- agricultural prices, land use, livestock, tillage, rural credit, and export access should matter
- industry cannot expand without labour movement, power, transport, imports, and urban capacity
- rural support should affect elections, mobilisation, and food policy

### Protection and industrialisation

`H` Fianna Fáil industrial policy encouraged protected domestic manufacturing and greater Irish control.

`P` A stronger Lemass programme could accelerate state companies, technical education, electricity, transport, and planned industry earlier than occurred.

Design direction:

- industrialisation should consume imported machinery, foreign exchange, skilled labour, and construction capacity
- protectionism can build capacity while lowering efficiency and export access
- foreign investment can accelerate growth while creating ownership and dependence disputes

### The Economic War

`H` Trade retaliation damaged sectors tied to the British market and shaped the politics of agriculture and finance.

Design direction:

- continued confrontation should strengthen economic sovereignty only if the player builds alternate markets and domestic processing
- settlement should restore trade but create political arguments about compromise

### Energy and turf

`H` Wartime fuel shortages increased the importance of domestic turf and emergency substitutes.

Design direction:

- bog development, transport, labour, environmental strain, and poor fuel quality form a real emergency economy system
- domestic fuel reduces import pressure but cannot replace oil for every military and industrial use

### Shipping and external supply

`H` Irish Shipping and the merchant marine became essential to imports during the Emergency.

Design direction:

- ships, crews, insurance, charters, convoy risk, port capacity, and diplomatic clearances should determine supply security
- losses create rationing, factory shutdowns, and pressure to align with a belligerent

### Labour and wages

`H` Emergency controls, inflation, shortages, and industrial mobilisation created labour conflict.

Design direction:

- wage restraint supports supply and finance but weakens social consent
- arbitration can strengthen parliamentary labour
- repression can radicalise unions
- workers' committees can improve production locally while challenging state authority

### Women and the social order

`H` The constitution and social policy reflected a strong domestic ideal for women, while wartime labour needs created practical contradictions.

Design direction:

- routes may preserve restrictive social policy, mobilise women through auxiliary and industrial work, expand professional service, or tie women's organisations to church, labour, republican, or defence networks
- these choices should affect manpower, production, social consent, and postwar reform

### Emigration and migration

`H` Migration to Britain and Northern Ireland connected Irish labour to the wider wartime economy.

Design direction:

- emigration can relieve unemployment and generate remittances
- it also removes manpower and skilled labour
- belligerent labour agreements create neutrality and sovereignty disputes

## Church, education, censorship, and language

### Church influence

`H` Catholic institutions had major social power without constituting a formal state church.

Design direction:

- clerical support can increase social consent and service delivery
- clerical vetoes can block labour, family, education, and pluralist reforms
- excessive political dependence can weaken state autonomy and minority confidence

### Education

`H` Education was deeply shaped by denominational institutions and language policy.

Design direction:

- technical education supports industrialisation and defence
- Irish-language education supports cultural goals and hidden route conditions
- centralisation creates conflict with religious managers and local communities

### Censorship

`H` Wartime censorship was organised through emergency powers and sought to preserve neutrality, control military information, and manage public morale.

Design direction:

- censorship can conceal cooperation, subversion, losses, and shortages
- strict censorship reduces foreign incidents and intelligence leakage
- it also creates rumours, opposition, and route-specific abuse

### Irish language

`H` The Constitution made Irish the first official language. State and cultural institutions promoted revival, but daily administration and economic life remained heavily English-speaking.

Design direction:

- language policy should affect education, civil service access, military terminology, broadcasting, Gaeltacht development, and cultural legitimacy
- coercive language policy can increase cultural radicalism while reducing administrative capacity and minority consent
- pragmatic bilingual policy can strengthen institutions while disappointing purists

## Neutrality as a strategic system

Neutrality must include at least seven policy dimensions.

### Constitutional authority

`H` Dáil assent was required for war participation. Emergency provisions could operate during an external conflict even when Ireland was not a belligerent.

Gameplay direction:

- entering war requires political preparation or emergency override
- abuse of emergency law moves the internal struggle and may close democratic routes

### Territorial denial

`H` Ireland had obligations to prevent belligerent use of its territory and waters.

Gameplay direction:

- internment, patrol, aircraft handling, ship repair, intelligence, and border access create repeated incidents

### Covert cooperation

`H` Irish, British, and American officers discussed invasion contingencies. Weather and other technical cooperation assisted the Allies.

Gameplay direction:

- cooperation improves readiness and access to equipment
- discovery reduces Neutrality Integrity and can empower republican or pro-Axis opposition

### Trade dependence

`H` Ireland depended on external shipping, imported fuel, machinery, and many goods.

Gameplay direction:

- belligerents can pressure trade, shipping, insurance, and access
- neutral credibility has economic as well as diplomatic value

### Intelligence and counterintelligence

`H` Ireland monitored belligerent representatives, subversive groups, communications, and coastal activity.

Gameplay direction:

- the state can cooperate selectively, balance services, feed deception, or pursue strict exclusion
- each choice changes foreign leverage and domestic trust

### Human movement

`H` Irish citizens served in foreign forces, travelled for work, crossed the border, and interacted with wartime economies.

Gameplay direction:

- passports, enlistment, deserters, labour migration, refugees, and prisoners create recurring choices

### Public legitimacy

`H` Neutrality became a source of national unity and sovereign identity.

Gameplay direction:

- abandoning neutrality is possible but politically expensive
- defending neutrality successfully should provide a distinct route conclusion, not a passive default ending

## Military research frame

### Starting weakness

`H` The permanent army was small and under-equipped after 1930s retrenchment.

Design direction:

- starting penalties should affect equipment, command, anti-air, motorisation, communications, and mobilisation capacity
- the player can solve these problems through several political philosophies

### Army models

1. `H` professional regular expansion
2. `H` local defence and volunteer mobilisation
3. `P` integrated guerrilla defence using republican networks under state command
4. `P` militarised corporatist or revolutionary mass mobilisation
5. `A` mythic host mobilisation in hidden routes

Each model should use different templates, costs, command risks, and political consequences.

### Coastal defence

`H` Coastwatching, ports, patrol craft, mine clearance, communications, and fixed defences were all real needs.

Design direction:

- named coastal regions should receive observation, signal, anti-air, coastal fort, patrol, and emergency response objectives
- a few strong ports cannot substitute for island-wide observation

### Air defence

`H` Ireland had limited aircraft and anti-air capacity.

Design direction:

- radar, observer networks, airfields, foreign purchases, pilot training, and neutral-airspace procedures should form a branch and decision family

### Naval and merchant distinction

`H` The marine defence force and merchant shipping served different purposes.

Design direction:

- naval patrol protects neutrality and clears mines
- merchant shipping protects the economy
- routes may merge command, keep civilian control, invite foreign escort, or accept losses

### Operational planning

`H` Irish defence plans considered invasion and cooperation against Axis attack.

`P` More extensive plans can include British intervention, independent redoubts, republican auxiliary networks, evacuation, scorched infrastructure, or a pre-emptive border move under specific routes.

Design direction:

- operational plans should create map missions and tied-down units
- plans must be chosen before invasion and cannot all be maximised at once

## Anglo-Irish relations

### Economic relationship

`H` Britain remained the dominant trade partner and principal external market despite political conflict.

### Defence relationship

`H` Britain viewed Irish ports, airspace, and territory through Atlantic and home-defence strategy. Ireland viewed British access as a sovereignty risk.

### Commonwealth relationship

`H` Ireland's constitutional ties to the Commonwealth had been weakened but not erased instantly in the 1930s.

`P` Fine Gael or Dillon could seek a cooperative Commonwealth settlement without restoring direct British rule or inventing a popular monarchist movement.

### Unity bargaining

`H` Britain floated a wartime unity proposal in 1940. The documented proposal was politically and practically uncertain.

`P` A successful bargain requires a far stronger negotiated package, consent process, guarantees, and security transition than Britain offered historically.

Design red line:

No route may portray Britain as able to hand over Northern Ireland without the Northern government, unionist population, military situation, and constitutional consequences mattering.

## Northern Ireland research frame

### Government and unionism

`H` Northern Ireland had a devolved unionist government and close constitutional ties to Britain.

`H` Unionist political dominance was reinforced by electoral rules, local-government boundary changes, patronage, security institutions, and the political organisation of the Protestant majority.

Design direction:

- unionism has internal factions, including hard security positions, pragmatic wartime administration, labour unionism, and reformist or federal possibilities
- resistance to Dublin control should be expected and organised

### Nationalist minority

`H` Northern nationalists faced political marginalisation and discrimination disputes while retaining local organisations, church networks, and a thirty-two-county identity.

Design direction:

- northern nationalist support is not automatically identical to IRA support
- constitutional nationalists, labour activists, clergy, community organisations, and militants should respond differently to southern actions

### Industry

`H` Belfast and other northern centres had shipbuilding, engineering, aircraft, textiles, and munitions capacity that expanded under wartime demand.

Design direction:

- reunification changes the southern economy dramatically
- industrial integration requires power, trade, labour settlement, management, finance, and access to markets
- war damage and strike politics must be considered

### Labour

`H` Trade union membership and industrial action grew during wartime mobilisation.

Design direction:

- cross-sectarian labour cooperation is possible but fragile
- labour routes can build a different unity coalition from nationalist or military routes

### Belfast Blitz

`H` The raids exposed weak civil defence and created large civilian losses and displacement.

Design direction:

- southern firefighting, medical aid, refugees, propaganda, intelligence, and diplomatic signalling should be event and mission content
- aid can build cross-border trust without erasing political division

### Conscription

`H` Proposals to extend conscription to Northern Ireland caused resistance and were connected to wider manpower debates.

Design direction:

- conscription pressure can align groups that otherwise disagree
- Dublin can intervene diplomatically, covertly, or through propaganda
- an aggressive route can exploit the crisis at the cost of foreign escalation

### Unity settlement requirements

Any non-absurd settlement needs a plan for:

1. constitutional authority
2. policing and security forces
3. unionist rights and local government
4. nationalist representation
5. religious and educational protections
6. industrial ownership and trade
7. public debt and taxation
8. military command and ports
9. civil service retention

## Historical and ethical red lines

1. Fascist routes must identify their authoritarianism, antisemitism, coercion, and anti-democratic content in the design direction. Cultural nationalism must not be used to conceal those features.
2. The Church must not be portrayed as one perfectly unified conspiratorial actor. Institutional power and internal differences both matter.
3. The IRA must not be treated as synonymous with all republicanism or northern nationalism.
4. Fine Gael must not be written as a default fascist party because of the Blueshirt episode.
5. Labour must not be written as an imported communist bloc without the political work needed to create that outcome.
6. Northern unionists must not vanish when Dublin chooses unity. Their institutions, fears, political power, and resistance require gameplay.
7. Northern nationalists must not be treated as passive territory markers. Their local politics and choices matter.
8. Neutrality must not be reduced to moral cowardice or perfect impartiality. It was a sovereignty policy with practical compromises and consequences.
9. British policy must not be written as omnipotent. Britain faced military, political, and constitutional limits.
10. German plans and contacts must not be treated as proof that Germany could easily invade, supply, or liberate Ireland.
11. Historical cultural revival must be separated from deliberate supernatural content.
12. Real suffering, including bombing, repression, executions, sectarian discrimination, and wartime deprivation, must not become cheap comedy.

# Part 3 research supplement: radical movements, cultural institutions, and concealed restoration material

## Research purpose and classification discipline

This supplement supports Part 3 of `ireland_focus_tree`. It covers the IRA, Republican Congress, Eoin O'Duffy and the National Corporate Party, Ailtirí na hAiséirghe, Gaelic revival organisations, folklore collection, royal sites, provincial traditions, learned law, and source-safe mythological material.

Every finding is assigned one of three design classifications.

- `H` means documented history, documented organisation, documented programme, documented institution, or documented cultural tradition.
- `P` means a plausible divergence that grows from a documented actor, institution, dispute, or campaign pressure.
- `A` means deliberate absurdity. It begins only after an explicit reveal threshold and must never be described as documented history.

The research standard for radical politics is conservative. A real movement can provide names, factions, programmes, symbols, and organisational problems. Its historical existence does not prove that it could seize the state without a severe crisis. The research standard for hidden cultural content is stricter. Folklore, medieval texts, place traditions, and royal sites can support serious policy and fictional route design. They do not establish supernatural events or an unbroken constitutional right to rule.

## IRA leadership and organisational research

### Organisational condition at the 1936 start

The IRA remains a real clandestine organisation with veteran prestige, local commands, safe houses, arms contacts, republican families, prisoners, and support networks. Its strategic direction is contested. The creation of Fianna Fáil, the transfer of republican voters into constitutional politics, state repression, internal disputes, and the departure of left republicans have narrowed its political base.

The route therefore opens with four different facts at once.

1. The organisation can disrupt public order and maintain a revolutionary claim.
2. It lacks a normal parliamentary path to government.
3. Its national leadership cannot assume obedience from every local command.
4. Its capacity to act is larger than its capacity to govern.

Part 3 converts those facts into Army Council Cohesion, Local Command Compliance, Public Republican Legitimacy, and Security Exposure. These values are distinct because an organisation can be cohesive while unpopular, popular while penetrated, or active while unable to supply its commands.

### Maurice Twomey

Maurice or Moss Twomey is the strongest elder-organiser option for a cautious reconstruction route. His career supports a role as an experienced republican authority who can reconnect commands, preserve organisational memory, and broker a convention. His release in December 1937 and later adjutant-general role under Russell make him useful as a guarantor or rival rather than an automatic 1936 chief of state.

Design classification:

- `H`: senior IRA organiser, former chief of staff, later adjutant-general, continuing republican authority.
- `P`: convention guarantor, reconstruction chair, or civil-military mediator after state breakdown.
- disallowed shortcut: automatic national leadership without a route process.

### Tom Barry

Tom Barry provides a credible military prestige route with stronger concern for strategy and political consequences. His brief service as chief of staff in 1937 and opposition to Russell's bombing strategy support an alternative convention before the S-Plan coalition wins.

Design classification:

- `H`: senior veteran, brief chief of staff, opponent of the proposed bombing campaign.
- `P`: professionalisation route, limited northern defence doctrine, or military guarantor of a civil republican compact.
- disallowed shortcut: treating War of Independence prestige as proof of mass support or modern military capacity.

### Seán Russell

Russell represents central command, the S-Plan, foreign contact, and a willingness to treat Britain as the main operational theatre. His route requires leadership victory, operational preparation, and tolerance for retaliation. German contact creates dependence, intelligence exposure, and strategic conflict. It cannot simply grant equipment or alliance benefits.

Design classification:

- `H`: IRA chief of staff, S-Plan leader, contacts with Germany, death during a submarine return voyage in August 1940.
- `P`: successful return, wartime restoration, or a state bid built around the S-Plan faction.
- disallowed shortcut: an Axis client route without domestic command and legitimacy requirements.

### Stephen Hayes

Hayes inherits a damaged organisation during Russell's absence. The 1941 crisis around his conduct, detention, and internal trial provides a direct model for a route where intelligence fear and factional conflict become inseparable. Part 3 uses this as a legitimacy and succession crisis rather than treating one historical accusation as a settled game fact.

Design classification:

- `H`: acting chief, internal arrest, court process, and deep organisational dispute.
- `P`: exoneration, negotiated retirement, rescue, command split, or security exposure crisis.
- design caution: events must describe competing accusations and evidence without presenting disputed claims as certain.

### Hugh McAteer, Charles Kerins, and later underground leadership

McAteer and Kerins support later Emergency succession after arrests and suppression remove earlier leaders. Their use should follow date, command survival, and regional organisation. They are not interchangeable generic chiefs.

- McAteer supports a northern-command emphasis, escape, prison, border organisation, and partition pressure.
- Kerins supports a later underground reconstruction shaped by severe losses, clandestine finance, and a smaller operational core.

### Jim O'Donovan and operational planning

Jim O'Donovan's role in drafting the S-Plan supports a specialist office for operations, communications, and foreign contact. It does not require him to become head of government. Part 3 keeps operations, public authority, head of state, and movement leadership as separate offices.

### Local commands and national orders

The IRA route should model district and brigade variation. Dublin, Cork, Munster, Connacht, border counties, Belfast, and other northern areas face different recruitment, security, supply, and political conditions. A national order can produce five outcomes.

1. compliance
2. delayed compliance
3. local adaptation
4. refusal
5. independent action

This is why the route needs map missions and Local Command Compliance. A focus cannot turn a clandestine organisation into a uniform army.

## Republican Congress research

### Founding split and decline

Republican Congress grows from the left opposition inside the IRA in 1934. Peadar O'Donnell, George Gilmore, Frank Ryan, Michael Price, and others sought a broad movement connecting republicanism, labour, tenants, unemployed workers, small farmers, anti-fascists, and northern working-class politics. The movement divided over whether it should become a new revolutionary party or a broad congress. It declined before 1936 and lost further organisational capacity through emigration, internal division, and the Spanish Civil War.

This history creates a reconstruction route with real limits.

- Congress cannot begin as a national party.
- It needs surviving organisers, local committees, trade-union links, a social programme, and a new founding congress.
- It cannot absorb the Labour Party by focus.
- It cannot treat all communists, socialists, republicans, tenants, and smallholders as one faction.

### Peadar O'Donnell

O'Donnell supports a plural social-republican route rooted in land, smallholders, cooperatives, rural debt, labour, anti-fascism, and republican legitimacy. He is a natural movement chair or public organiser. He is not an automatic command dictator.

### George Gilmore

Gilmore supports the broad-front and organising wing. His earlier IRA connections, Congress role, and cooperation with Ryan and O'Donnell make him useful for local committees, anti-sectarian work, and negotiation with labour actors.

### Frank Ryan

Ryan provides military prestige, anti-fascist credibility, and a difficult foreign-return problem. His historical imprisonment, transfer to Germany, and attempted submarine return with Russell mean that any Irish return must account for health, German control, his own politics, and public suspicion. A returned Ryan can bridge defence and revolutionary politics only after a route event resolves those issues.

### Michael Price and revolutionary-party pressure

Michael Price supports the more centralised revolutionary-party tendency. This creates the main internal route conflict between plural congress authority and disciplined vanguard control. The conflict must affect committee mandates, union autonomy, elections, security, and agrarian participation.

### Roddy Connolly, Nora Connolly O'Brien, Seán Murray, and communist organisation

These figures support communist, labour, anti-fascist, and Connolly inheritance interfaces. Their participation is conditional. The Communist Party, Labour affiliates, trade unions, and republican committees retain different organisations and strategic priorities. Soviet or international communist contact cannot substitute for domestic support.

### Labour and trade-union boundary

The parliamentary Labour route remains complete unless a named breakdown occurs. A workers' republic becomes possible only when several conditions align.

- repeated failure of parliamentary government formation or reform
- Labour Organisation at least decisive
- a split or radical caucus inside labour and affiliated unions
- surviving Republican Congress organisers
- local committees with a valid mandate

### Northern and anti-sectarian dimension

Congress history supports a route that treats class cooperation and anti-sectarian organisation as a real objective. Part 4 must preserve northern labour, nationalist, unionist, British, and local-government actors separately.

## Ailtirí na hAiséirghe research

### Concealed emergence

Gearóid Ó Cuinneagáin worked through pro-Axis underground circles during the early Emergency and then created Craobh na hAiséirghe under the cover of a Gaelic League branch. The branch developed a wider network and attempted to use respected cultural organisation as a political springboard. This supports a concealed pre-party phase built around cells, publications, classes, youth work, and organisational capture.

The route must preserve a hard boundary between serious language organisations and fascist infiltration. Conradh na Gaeilge does not automatically become Ailtirí. Ailtirí must capture, split, or leave cultural institutions through explicit events.

### Public launch and programme

The public party was launched in June 1942. Its programme supported one-party rule under a Ceannaire, a seven-year concentration of power, state-directed economic development, compulsory language transformation, criminalisation of emigration, discrimination against Jews and freemasons, mass conscription, and reconquest of Northern Ireland.

These are core route facts. They require explicit coercion, exclusion, antisemitism, censorship, militarisation, and damage to minority confidence. They cannot be softened into a generic nationalist route.

### Organisational reach

Historical work reports a substantial Craobh network in the early Emergency and about 60 Ailtirí branches by 1945, with organisation on both sides of the border. Membership and branch count did not create a national majority. The route therefore uses Cellular Reach, Cadre Discipline, patronage, local elections, publications, and organiser quality instead of party popularity alone.

### Patrons and mainstream interfaces

J. J. Walsh, Ernest Blythe, Oliver J. Flanagan, and other sympathetic or adjacent figures show that Ailtirí could gain help from people outside its formal membership. These relationships must remain specific.

- money and material support
- press access
- advice on organisation or constitution
- parliamentary questions or publicity
- refusal to join the party
- strategic disagreement

A patron does not become a party leader automatically.

### Leadership conflict

Tomás Ó Dochartaigh supported tactical cooperation and stronger organisation. Ó Cuinneagáin suspended leading critics in September 1945 and expelled them in October, damaging the movement. The route must include the split, with possible outcomes involving leader victory, organiser victory, negotiated directorate, party collapse, or state seizure before the dispute is resolved.

### Foreign view

Ailtirí can admire aspects of Axis states without becoming a simple German puppet. Ó Cuinneagáin presented Ireland as a future ideological model with global ambition. This supports a route conflict between foreign opportunists, ideological missionaries, clerical corporatists, cultural organisers, and hard totalists. German support remains conditional and dangerous.

### Post-Axis survival

The defeat of Germany should create a major route crisis. The party can deny, purge, adapt, radicalise, seek a neutral total-state identity, or collapse. It cannot continue unchanged after 1945 without costs.

## Royal sites and High Kingship material

### Tara

Heritage Ireland describes the Hill of Tara as a place of long significance and as the seat of the high kings in Irish tradition. The Lia Fáil is presented as a coronation stone and one of the legendary treasures of the Tuatha Dé Danann. The physical site, monuments, later symbolism, and association with inauguration provide strong serious cultural content.

The design boundary is clear.

- `H`: heritage protection, archaeology, public ceremony, historical study, symbolic presidency, constitutional debate, and use of Tara as a national site.
- `P`: an elected guardian crown, ceremonial High Kingship, royal convention, regency, or constitutional office created during a crisis.
- `A`: a stone that verifies a ruler, binding geasa, returned treasures, sacral sovereignty, or impossible authority.

No genealogical claim alone can grant government. A plausible crown requires Dáil action, provincial assent, public ratification, law, finance, and succession rules.

### Other royal and inauguration sites

Uisneach, Cashel, Cruachan, Dún Ailinne, Emain Macha, Tailteann, and other sites can provide regional missions, cultural institutions, and competing claims. Their historical roles differ. Part 6 research must source each site's exact association before final text or symbols are assigned.

## Five Provinces and Mide material

The Irish term for province is commonly connected with a fifth part, and educational heritage material records Meath or Mide as a fifth province. The boundaries and political systems of early Ireland changed over time. Part 3 must not present one fixed pentarchy as a timeless constitution.

The design boundary is:

- `H`: four modern provinces, historical Mide, provincial identities, royal sites, regional organisations, and changing over-kingdoms.
- `P`: a modern federal or confederal convention that uses five regional mandates and restores a middle district for mediation or administration.
- `A`: a literal pentarchy with sovereign provincial hosts, impossible fifth-province centrality, or provincial institutions that acquire supernatural force.

Ulster creates the hardest problem. Historical Ulster is not the same as the six-county state, modern province, unionist electorate, nationalist population, or a single cultural community. A five-province settlement must define who speaks for Ulster and how people outside state control participate. It cannot annex Northern Ireland by declaring a province.

## Learned law and Brehon material

Brehon law describes historical Irish legal traditions that were recorded, developed, and later displaced. Modern legal sources discuss features such as arbitration and fines. These sources can support serious study of legal history, mediation, compensation, and scholarly institutions.

The design boundary is:

- `H`: translation, publication, university study, legal history, arbitration research, and cultural education.
- `P`: advisory customary courts, restorative local panels, or a constitutional commission adapting selected principles under modern rights law.
- `A`: learned orders whose judgements bind rulers through impossible sanction, living legal manuscripts, or courts recognised by Otherworld actors.

The route must not romanticise historical law as a complete modern rights code. Gender, status, property, religion, class, and due-process questions require explicit modern safeguards.

## Folklore and Otherworld material

UCD describes strong traditions concerning a parallel fairy world, the sí, and beings associated with raths, liosanna, and cairns. The tradition contains fear, respect, euphemistic names, local explanations, music, encounters, death, weather, and place-specific belief. The National Folklore Collection preserves regional variation rather than one fixed supernatural system.

This material supports serious policy before any absurd reveal.

- protect archaeological and locally sensitive sites
- collect testimony with consent
- prevent fraud and exploitation
- investigate accidents, smuggling, espionage, weather, and public panic
- preserve local language and names
- compare independent records
- distinguish pastoral, scientific, security, and scholarly responses

The route crosses into `A` only after the specified evidence chain produces an impossible result. The design must continue to acknowledge regional variation. Tuatha, Fianna, sí, returned heroes, guardians, and local court actors cannot be reduced to one homogeneous faction.

1. Folklore is attributed to tradition, collection, text, or place. It is not described as verified history.

9. Generated symbols begin only after the route becomes fictional or impossible. Real people, flags, manuscripts, sites, and attested movement symbols require source work.

## The material economy before the Emergency

### Agricultural dependence and trade exposure

The southern state entered 1936 with a predominantly agricultural economy, a narrow domestic market, low industrial capacity, and deep exposure to trade with Britain. The Economic War had imposed heavy costs on farming and trade. The agreements of 25 April 1938 settled financial, trade, and defence disputes, including a one-time payment of £10 million and the transfer of the retained harbour defences. The settlement eased immediate pressure without removing structural dependence on British markets, imported coal, fertiliser, machinery, shipping, and credit.

Design consequences:

- The 1938 settlement raises sovereignty and trade access while exposing the defence and supply cost of independent control.
- Protectionism can support young industries, but it cannot create machinery, fuel, skills, or export markets by decree.
- Agricultural recovery depends on prices, transport, farm organisation, fertiliser, seed, machinery, credit, and access to buyers.
- British trade remains economically valuable even when political relations are hostile.
- A route that breaks trade must replace food exports, coal imports, fertiliser, shipping, and foreign exchange through concrete systems.

### Protection and industrial policy

Seán Lemass used the Department of Industry and Commerce to promote protected industry and state-led development. The Department created an industrial development branch in 1932. Tariffs and licensing encouraged domestic production, though investment, scale, technology, finance, and cabinet resistance limited the programme. Wartime service as Minister for Supplies exposed the gap between nominal industrial sovereignty and dependence on imported inputs.

Design consequences:

- Early Lemass development is a protected and administratively driven project, not the later export-led programme moved backward unchanged.
- Domestic industry needs electricity, transport, imported machine tools, skilled labour, construction materials, and demand.
- Industrial licensing can build capacity and also create patronage, protected inefficiency, and urban concentration.
- Technical institutes, standards, laboratories, procurement, and state-company governance matter as much as factory counts.
- A successful developmental route should emerge from repeated delivery and institutional learning.

### State companies and strategic infrastructure

The Shannon hydroelectric scheme and the Electricity Supply Board provided an existing national electrical institution. The broad rural electrification programme began in 1946, after the core wartime period. Part 4 can allow earlier preparation, surveys, pilot districts, local distribution projects, and route-specific acceleration. It cannot present universal rural electrification in 1936 as documented history.

The Turf Development Board was established in 1934 to develop peat resources. Emergency fuel scarcity expanded the political importance of turf. The wartime experience contributed to the decision to build an industrial peat programme, leading to Bord na Móna under the Turf Development Act 1946.

Design consequences:

- Electricity begins as a real national network with uneven reach and limited spare capacity.
- Rural electrification before 1946 is `P` and requires surveys, capital, poles, wire, substations, trained crews, local payment capacity, and regional sequencing.
- Peat can replace part of imported fuel demand. It brings moisture, transport, labour, machinery, seasonal production, and environmental costs.
- Turf is poorly suited to some industrial and locomotive uses. Fuel substitution should lower collapse risk without acting as universal coal.
- Postwar ESB and Bord na Móna expansion are strong late-game conclusions rooted in wartime experience.

## Emergency supply, food, and rationing

### Department of Supplies

The Department of Supplies, with Lemass as minister and John Leydon as its senior administrative figure, managed wartime scarcity, allocation, price controls, rationing, imports, shipping, and substitution. It became a central economic institution because war disrupted normal markets and exposed the state's weak supply position.

Design consequences:

- Emergency control is a real administrative system with staff, information, enforcement, exemptions, appeals, and corruption risk.
- Controls can conserve scarce goods and damage consent through delays, unequal access, black markets, or patronage.
- Route-specific governments inherit the supply apparatus. They may reform, democratise, militarise, corporatise, provincialise, or capture it.
- Supply administration raises Administrative Reach when competently delivered and lowers it when orders exceed information or enforcement capacity.
- The apparatus must have an expiry or transition after the war. Permanent economic emergency is a political conclusion, not free efficiency.

### Compulsory tillage and food production

Compulsory tillage required farmers to place specified land under crops. It aimed to protect domestic food supply and reduce dependence on imports. Enforcement generated resentment and resistance, especially when quotas ignored local soil, labour, machinery, livestock, or farm scale. Guaranteed prices and propaganda accompanied coercive measures.

Design consequences:

- Tillage is a mission family with county quotas, seed, fertiliser, machinery, transport, weather, and enforcement choices.
- The player can pursue negotiated quotas, guaranteed prices, cooperative machinery, inspector enforcement, or requisition.
- Food output can rise while Rural Organisation, Social Consent, or livestock production falls.
- Partial success is common. Grain targets may be met while animal feed, farm income, or regional trust deteriorates.
- Clann na Talmhan, farmer independents, Labour, vocational councils, IRA district commands, Congress committees, and authoritarian regimes respond differently.

### Rationing and price controls

Rationing covered fuel, food, clothing, and other scarce goods in changing combinations. Controls continued beyond the formal end of the war, with later orders revoking or replacing individual price and supply instruments. The Emergency economy therefore needs a postwar unwinding phase rather than an instant return to normal trade.

Design consequences:

- Rationing is a living policy package linked to stocks, imports, harvests, transport, administrative reach, and black-market pressure.
- Equal formal rations can produce unequal real access because distribution, income, location, and influence differ.
- Urban and rural shortages should not use identical effects.
- Church service networks, unions, cooperatives, local government, merchants, and security services can improve distribution or capture it.
- Demobilisation and control removal are timed political choices with inflation, unemployment, and legitimacy consequences.

## Shipping, ports, and Maritime Access

### Irish Shipping

Irish Shipping Limited was created in March 1941 after the loss and withdrawal of commercial tonnage made essential imports increasingly precarious. The company assembled whatever vessels could be acquired and became a strategic instrument of neutral supply. Its formation did not remove danger, scarce tonnage, insurance, crew, repair, navigation, or foreign-pressure problems.

Design consequences:

- A merchant marine is built through ship acquisition, chartering, repair, insurance, crews, port turnaround, and diplomatic access.
- Neutral markings reduce some risks but do not create immunity.
- Shipping losses remove actual capacity and trained crews. Replacement takes time and foreign exchange.
- Rescue actions can increase moral authority and foreign goodwill while diverting ships or exposing routes.
- Irish Shipping becomes a permanent postwar institution only if the route resolves debt, fleet renewal, commercial purpose, and state control.

### Port geography

Dublin, Cork Harbour, Waterford, Limerick and the Shannon Estuary, Galway, Sligo, Rosslare, and smaller western and southern harbours have different roles. Cork combines the transferred harbour defences with major commercial and naval potential. Dublin is the main administrative and import centre. Limerick and the Shannon connect western and midland supply. Waterford and Rosslare support eastern and southern trade. Galway and Sligo offer western access with limited capacity.

Design consequences:

- Port projects are regional and purpose-specific.
- Deep-water capacity, cranes, warehouses, fuel storage, rail connection, customs, repair, mine risk, and coastal defence are separate needs.
- A port cannot simultaneously maximise neutral commerce, secret military access, and low foreign leverage without safeguards.
- Northern settlement can add Belfast, Londonderry, Larne, and other northern facilities only after a political and administrative transition.

### Merchant risk and maritime incidents

Coast-watching records show the density of wartime air and sea activity around Ireland. Neutral shipping faced submarine, mine, aircraft, misidentification, weather, and insurance risk. Maritime policy therefore connects economy, neutrality, intelligence, rescue, and diplomacy.

Design consequences:

- Maritime Access is a component of National Provision.
- Shipping incidents enter the Neutrality Ledger with actor, cause, evidence, casualties, cargo, and response.
- Publicly blaming a belligerent can protect credibility and damage access.
- Concealing an incident can preserve a relationship and raise exposure or domestic distrust.
- Armed escort, convoy cooperation, routing intelligence, and port access each change neutrality and dependency.

## Transport, coal, and internal distribution

### Great Southern Railways and wartime strain

The Great Southern Railways operated most railways wholly within the state. The system entered the Emergency with weak finances and ageing assets. Imported coal shortages, poor substitute fuels, maintenance scarcity, increased freight demands, and reduced passenger services placed it under severe strain. Córas Iompair Éireann was created from the Great Southern Railways and the Dublin United Transport Company in 1945.

Design consequences:

- Rail capacity is a scarce network, not an abstract infrastructure bonus.
- Coal quality, locomotive maintenance, workshops, crews, spare parts, and timetables determine performance.
- Freight priority can protect food and fuel while damaging civilian travel, local markets, and labour mobility.
- Railhead distribution requires road vehicles, horses, warehouses, and local administration.
- CIÉ formation is a late-war or postwar institutional conclusion with governance, debt, labour, and service obligations.

### Roads, petrol, and controlled transport

Petrol imports fell sharply during the Emergency. Private and commercial road use was restricted, while essential transport received priority. Controlled-area schemes shifted goods toward railheads and coordinated distribution.

Design consequences:

- Petrol rationing changes construction, agriculture, policing, military mobility, and regional market access.
- Trucks are a real statecraft resource. Diverting them to the army weakens civilian distribution.
- Road projects improve local access but consume imported bitumen, machinery, fuel, labour, and construction capacity.
- Horse transport remains relevant and has fodder, veterinary, speed, and seasonal limits.
- Transport Serviceability must be evaluated across rail, road, ports, depots, and local delivery.

## Defence inheritance and the Treaty Ports

### The 1938 transfer

The harbour defences at Cobh, Berehaven, and Lough Swilly were transferred under the 1938 agreements. The Coastal Defence Artillery was established to take responsibility for the fortifications. Officers received training and inherited fixed weapons, magazines, emplacements, instruments, and maintenance burdens.

Design consequences:

- The transfer is a sovereignty gain and a readiness obligation.
- Forts begin with inherited equipment, limited trained crews, maintenance backlogs, and uneven operational value.
- Cork, Berehaven, and Lough Swilly need separate projects and political treatment.
- Lough Swilly creates a special northern and British strategic problem because the harbour is in Donegal while its approaches and regional war context involve Northern Ireland.
- Returning access to Britain, denying access, or creating joint defence must use the Neutrality Ledger and obligation safeguards.

### Emergency defence planning

The Military Archives hold Emergency Defence Plans for 1939 to 1946, unit journals, G2 files, coastal defence records, Air Defence Command records, censorship records, and northern intelligence collections. These sources show a state building plans and institutions under material limits.

Design consequences:

- National Readiness is composite. Manpower alone cannot represent defence.
- Plans can exist without equipment and equipment can exist without trained command, communications, supply, or political authority.
- Exercises expose weaknesses, consume stocks, improve command, and affect public confidence.
- An invasion plan must define mobilisation zones, supply, demolitions, liaison, ports, airfields, communications, and civil authority.

## Army, reserves, and local defence

### Mobilisation limits

Ireland expanded its defence forces during the Emergency and created local and auxiliary organisations. The state could raise personnel faster than it could provide modern weapons, transport, radios, anti-aircraft guns, aircraft, fuel, and trained officers.

Design consequences:

- Mobilisation Depth is one Readiness component among seven.
- Expanding units without Equipment Sufficiency or Logistics lowers average quality and raises supply burden.
- Reserve mobilisation has training, employment, farm-labour, family, housing, and equipment costs.
- Radical and authoritarian routes can compel service quickly. They still face desertion, disobedience, local capture, and supply failure.

### Local Security Force and Local Defence Force

The Local Security Force and Local Defence Force provide a historical foundation for territorial defence, local observation, guards, and reserve organisation. They must remain distinct from a fully equipped field army.

Design consequences:

- Local units can guard bridges, depots, coasts, airfields, communications, and local approaches.
- Local knowledge improves observation and mobilisation.
- Political reliability varies by county, route, commander, and security history.
- Heavy deployment can damage agriculture and essential work.
- Integration, autonomy, disbandment, or politicisation become route choices.

## Coast watching, air defence, and maritime security

### Coast Watching Service

Military Archives material records 83 Look Out Posts around the coast. Their logbooks tracked aircraft, shipping, weather, incidents, and unusual activity. The network fed information to Army Intelligence.

Design consequences:

- Coast watching is a national observation network with regional posts and communications.
- Building a hut does not create reliable information. Staffing, training, telephones, maps, recognition guides, weather exposure, and relief matter.
- Reports can support rescue, neutrality enforcement, shipping routes, air warning, intelligence, and foreign liaison.
- German, British, American, IRA, smuggling, and hidden-route interests can target the network.

### Air Defence Command

The Air Observer Corps became Air Defence Command. Military Archives records cover telephone coordination, foreign-aircraft reporting, Garda cooperation, Air Corps training, anti-aircraft guns, searchlights, signals, maps, meteorological reports, and Air and Marine Intelligence.

Design consequences:

- Air defence has observation, communication, identification, interception, and fire-control layers.
- Anti-aircraft guns without warning and communications have limited value.
- Searchlights, phones, radio, maps, training, and ammunition are concrete projects.
- Firing on foreign aircraft can protect sovereignty and create diplomatic incidents.
- Route-specific command rules determine who may authorise fire.

### Air Corps limits

The Air Corps offers training, liaison, reconnaissance, limited interception, and emergency response. It cannot become a major wartime air force through one branch. Aircraft acquisition depends on foreign release, payment, pilots, maintenance, fuel, ammunition, airfields, and spare parts.

Design consequences:

- The player can choose observation and liaison, point defence, maritime patrol, training depth, or foreign interoperability.
- Each role competes for scarce aircraft and crews.
- Captured, interned, purchased, or donated aircraft require evaluation and maintenance.
- Open Allied or Axis alignment can improve access and sharply increase dependence.

### Marine Service

The Emergency Marine Service and Air and Marine Intelligence provide the historical basis for harbour examination, coastal patrol, rescue, inspection, and limited maritime security. A full fleet requires postwar investment, shipbuilding, trained crews, doctrine, and a defined strategic purpose.

Design consequences:

- Early maritime defence focuses on examination vessels, mines, patrol craft, rescue, harbour control, and merchant support.
- Dockyards cannot produce a blue-water fleet without steel, engines, design, labour, and foreign access.
- Northern settlement can transform the naval question through Belfast industry and Atlantic bases.

## Intelligence, counter-intelligence, and censorship

### G2 and Special Branch

G2, Garda Special Branch, military intelligence, and censorship dealt with foreign agents, the IRA, northern reporting, shipping, aircraft, and wartime security. German agents and IRA contacts created real threats. British and American liaison offered information while increasing foreign access and exposure.

Design consequences:

- Intelligence has collection, analysis, liaison, counter-intelligence, security enforcement, and public-legitimacy dimensions.
- A route can improve collection while corrupting civilian oversight.
- Foreign liaison is source-specific and raises obligations.
- Republican governments inherit records and infiltrations rather than beginning with a clean service.
- Authoritarian governments can gain short-term penetration and damage reliability through politicised reporting.

### Emergency censorship in Part 4

The Office of the Controller of Censorship was a major Emergency institution. Censorship protected operational security and neutrality, limited propaganda, and influenced public information.

Design consequences:

- Censorship can lower intelligence exposure and incident escalation.
- It can also reduce Minority Confidence, Social Consent, press trust, and the credibility of official casualty or shortage reporting.
- Route laws should distinguish military censorship, neutrality censorship, party control, moral censorship, and secret-state suppression.
- Postwar retention creates a constitutional crisis or authoritarian conclusion.

## Active neutrality

### Broad political support

Contemporary diplomatic material described neutrality as supported by the major parties and an overwhelming share of the public. Neutrality was tied to sovereignty, partition, civil-war memory, military weakness, and fear that entry would split the country.

Design consequences:

- Neutrality cannot be removed by one focus or one leader preference.
- War entry requires readiness, public consent, parliamentary or route-valid authority, external safeguards, and a Northern policy.
- A foreign attack can change public consent and legal authority quickly, though material capacity remains limited.
- Covert cooperation can coexist with public neutrality when legal treatment and public doctrine remain credible.

### Legal consistency and belligerent treatment

Neutral states were expected to control territory, intern belligerent personnel and matériel, enforce rules, and prevent territory being used as a military base. Irish practice also contained pragmatic asymmetries and quiet cooperation.

Design consequences:

- Neutrality Integrity measures the consistency and public defensibility of law, capacity, and conduct, not identical treatment in every case.
- Internment, release, repair, rescue, weather, overflight, intelligence, and shipping access each have public rules and secret exceptions.
- Repeated unacknowledged exceptions raise exposure.
- Strict formal parity can preserve credibility and damage Allied relations or humanitarian outcomes.

### Weather cooperation

Met Éireann records show the importance of Blacksod observations to the Allied D-Day forecast in June 1944. This is a strong example of technical cooperation that carried major strategic value without public belligerency.

Design consequences:

- Weather reporting is an active neutrality policy domain.
- Reports can be open under international practice, restricted, selectively accelerated, falsified, or used in liaison.
- Accurate reporting improves scientific and maritime capacity.
- Secret priority access raises Allied leverage and exposure.
- Falsification damages professional institutions and can cause severe consequences.

### Air and maritime incidents

Foreign aircraft crossed, crashed, or landed in Ireland, while shipping and coastal waters experienced belligerent activity. The Coast Watching Service and Air Defence Command observed and reported incidents.

Design consequences:

- The Neutrality Ledger needs an incident queue with actor, location, evidence, severity, casualties, and response deadline.
- Some incidents are accidents, some are deliberate tests, and some are ambiguous.
- Rescue can be separated from military assistance.
- Return, internment, repair, concealment, protest, compensation, or escalation are distinct responses.

## British and American pressure

### British defence demands

British officials pressed for cooperation, access, joint defence, and the return or use of the former Treaty Ports. British policy also linked arms, supplies, invasion fears, and Northern Ireland to Irish decisions.

Design consequences:

- British Leverage rises through trade, arms, shipping, intelligence, access, and Northern guarantees.
- British access can improve readiness and reduce Neutrality Integrity if poorly governed.
- Irish bargaining can secure arms, trade, guarantees, liaison, and sovereignty clauses.
- A joint defence plan must define command, entry conditions, withdrawal, and treatment of Northern territory.

### United States pressure and diaspora politics

Documents on Irish Foreign Policy record strong pressure from American minister David Gray and broader American strategic concern. They also record Irish arguments based on public opinion, diaspora ties, partition, and supply pressure.

Design consequences:

- American Leverage is separate from British Leverage.
- Irish-American lobbying can improve access and political space, but it cannot command the United States government.
- Aid may come with basing, information, political, shipping, or alignment demands.
- A route can cultivate diaspora bonds, humanitarian work, investment, and recognition without formal war entry.
- Open anti-American propaganda can close aid and diaspora channels.

## German liaison and Axis opportunism

German intelligence activity and IRA contacts created security risks, while diplomatic relations continued under neutrality. German communications acknowledged that military operations might affect Irish interests. Axis opportunity did not provide a reliable route to Irish sovereignty or unity.

Design consequences:

- German Leverage can provide intelligence, agents, money, weapons proposals, recognition promises, or propaganda.
- Every benefit raises Security Exposure, British reaction, and client risk.
- Germany cannot transfer Northern Ireland through a diplomatic promise.
- A German or Axis victory does not erase occupation, racial, authoritarian, strategic, or dependency costs.
- Authoritarian routes still require Irish organisational capacity and a succession plan.

## Vatican, neutral, and small-state diplomacy

The Holy See offers moral, diplomatic, humanitarian, and Catholic-network connections. Vatican Leverage remains separate from domestic Church influence. Neutral states and small countries offer legal, trade, mediation, humanitarian, and postwar conference opportunities.

Design consequences:

- Vatican diplomacy can support humanitarian exchange, mediation, recognition, education, or regime legitimacy.
- It cannot directly command Irish bishops, services, voters, or cabinets.
- Neutral cooperation can cover shipping information, prisoner welfare, diplomatic representation, trade, and international law.
- Small-state leagues need concrete members, goals, and enforcement limits.

## Northern Ireland political structure

### Stormont and unionist government

Northern Ireland had a devolved parliament and government dominated by the Ulster Unionist Party. James Craig, J. M. Andrews, and Basil Brooke mark leadership transitions relevant to the wartime period. Stormont had its own ministries, parliament, police and security institutions, local-government system, and close constitutional ties to the United Kingdom.

Design consequences:

- Northern Ireland is an organised state structure, not an unadministered claim.
- Any settlement must decide the future of Stormont, ministries, civil servants, courts, police, local authorities, and UK law.
- Unionist parties and institutions do not disappear when London changes policy.
- A British offer cannot transfer effective control without northern implementation and consent or coercion.

### Nationalist representation and abstention

Nationalist politicians, including Joe Devlin and Cahir Healy, operated within and around the Northern parliament. Abstention, participation, local organisation, repression, and constitutional strategy varied over time.

Design consequences:

- A Northern Nationalist Consent must be earned through representation, organisations, local government, public campaigns, or crisis.
- Dublin cannot treat every northern nationalist as an agent of its current route.
- Revolutionary, Labour, royal, federal, and constitutional offers divide northern nationalists differently.

### Labour and civic politics

Wartime industrial growth expanded union organisation. Northern Ireland War Memorial research records trade-union membership rising from about 90,000 in 1938 to nearly 150,000 in 1945, along with major disputes in engineering and aircraft production.

Design consequences:

- Labour Cooperation is a separate Northern value that cannot be inferred from class rhetoric or party labels.
- Class cooperation can cross communal lines and can also fracture over constitution, security, jobs, and wartime production.
- Congress, Labour, vocational, corporate, and constitutional routes require different northern labour bargains.
- Industrial action and state repression have production, legitimacy, and security effects.

### Electoral and local-government disputes

Northern parliamentary and local electoral systems, constituency boundaries, property qualifications, plural voting, housing, employment, and security law generated long-running discrimination disputes. Historians differ over the scale and mechanism in particular institutions. The evidence supports modelling unequal confidence, contested legitimacy, and institutional reform without inventing a simple universal statistic.

Design consequences:

- Plebiscites require franchise rules, district boundaries, registration, security, campaign freedom, observers, and dispute resolution.
- Local-government reform can raise Nationalist Consent and Labour Cooperation while alarming unionist institutions.
- A county vote cannot settle Belfast neighbourhoods, border districts, or mixed industrial corridors by itself.
- Minority rights are a continuing system after any territorial result.

## Northern Ireland wartime economy and defence

### Strategic industry

Harland and Wolff, Short and Harland, engineering works, ropeworks, linen, ports, and related firms made Northern Ireland a major wartime industrial region. Northern Ireland War Memorial research records employment of roughly 30,000 at Harland and Wolff in 1942 and growth at Short and Harland from about 4,000 employees in 1938 to about 12,000 in early 1942.

Design consequences:

- Belfast industry is valuable and highly specialised.
- Integration requires contracts, managers, labour, electricity, imported steel, British orders, finance, and protection.
- Seizing Belfast does not create usable dockyards or aircraft production.
- Bombing, strikes, sabotage, flight of skilled labour, and broken supply chains can reduce output sharply.
- A negotiated settlement can preserve British orders or convert capacity gradually.

### Belfast Blitz and cross-border relief

The German raids of 1941 killed large numbers of civilians, destroyed housing and services, and disrupted industry. The Easter Tuesday raid caused almost 750 deaths. Later raids caused further deaths and serious industrial and civic disruption. Fire brigades from the southern state assisted after the attacks.

Design consequences:

- The Belfast Blitz is a major event chain with humanitarian, neutrality, industrial, Northern, and diplomatic effects.
- Southern aid can raise civic cooperation and expose the practical value of all-island coordination.
- The event cannot act as a free unity trigger.
- Failure to aid, exploitative propaganda, or sectarian relief can damage long-term settlement prospects.
- Industrial recovery requires dispersal, repairs, labour, housing, and air defence.

### Conscription and wartime status

Northern Ireland participated in the British war effort without the same conscription regime applied in Great Britain. Conscription proposals were politically explosive and interacted with nationalist resistance, labour, British strategy, and security.

Design consequences:

- Conscription pressure is a Northern mission and crisis, not a fixed background modifier.
- Dublin routes can oppose, exploit, negotiate, or condition assistance.
- Unionist government can seek manpower without sacrificing constitutional control.
- Labour and nationalist reactions vary by safeguards and war situation.

## Northern settlement history and plausible routes

### Boundary and partition memory

The Boundary Commission settlement, Articles 2 and 3 of the 1937 Constitution, Treaty debates, local demographic claims, and repeated diplomatic proposals shaped all later unity politics. No single historical episode supplies an uncontested territorial formula.

Design consequences:

- County, district, federal, unitary, and deferred settlements are separate route families.
- Territorial claims do not equal administrative control or cores.
- Border communities, Belfast, Derry, Fermanagh, Tyrone, Armagh, Down, and Antrim require distinct treatment.
- Foreign guarantees and UK parliamentary action remain relevant.

### The 1940 British proposals

Documents on Irish Foreign Policy record British proposals accepting the principle of unity, a joint committee, and a joint defence council during the 1940 crisis. De Valera rejected a vague future principle and argued for immediate united neutrality, arms, and sovereign control. British officials stressed unionist opposition and the wartime value of Northern Ireland.

Design consequences:

- The 1940 route is a complex wartime bargaining chain.
- The offer has British, Stormont, Dublin, public, military, and American components.
- Acceptance begins negotiation. It does not transfer the North.
- Failure can still produce joint planning, humanitarian coordination, or a clearer lawful deferment.
- A route that exploits British weakness without Northern preparation risks conflict and occupation.

### Federal and consent-based settlement

Federal ideas can reconcile regional institutions and all-island sovereignty only if powers, finance, rights, security, and guarantees are negotiated. Provincial symbolism from Part 3 can inform a modern arrangement without substituting mythic geography for consent.

Design consequences:

- A federal route must specify Stormont or successor assembly powers, central competences, taxation, courts, police, defence, language, religion, education, welfare, and vetoes.
- Unionist Consent, Nationalist Consent, Labour Cooperation, British Flexibility, Security Stability, and Integration Capacity each matter.
- A plebiscite route requires lawful and credible rules before the vote.
- Coercion produces occupation and long integration, not immediate cores.

## Postwar reconstruction and constitutional change

### Demobilisation and controls

The end of the European war did not instantly remove shortages or Emergency controls. Individual price, supply, shipping, exchange, and administrative measures continued, changed, or were re-enacted in later legislation.

Design consequences:

- Demobilisation releases manpower and creates unemployment, training, housing, pension, and political pressures.
- Controls can be retained temporarily, converted into peacetime planning, or dismantled.
- A route must balance reconstruction, inflation, employment, trade, and legitimacy.
- Emergency law expiry is part of every conclusion.

### Republic declaration

The Republic of Ireland Act 1948 came into operation on 18 April 1949. It provides a historical late-game constitutional endpoint for Costello's inter-party government and a plausible alternative endpoint for other routes under different procedures.

Design consequences:

- The republic declaration is not a generic focus reward.
- It requires the correct government, constitutional process, external-relations settlement, diplomatic preparation, and country-identity package.
- Royal and hidden crown routes require different external arrangements.
- Commonwealth, British, American, Vatican, northern, and domestic reactions depend on the route and settlement.

### Postwar development

CIÉ, Bord na Móna, rural electrification, fleet renewal, housing, public health, industrial policy, and European reconstruction provide grounded late-game projects. Ireland's small size and neutrality create diplomatic openings and strategic limits.

Ireland joined the United Nations on 14 December 1955. This lies beyond the core 1936 to 1949 political sequence, but it provides a documented late-game endpoint for multilateral small-state diplomacy and for routes that preserve a recognised, functioning state.

Design consequences:

- Postwar branches transform wartime institutions into development, social provision, or permanent command systems.
- Foreign loans and markets can accelerate recovery and create leverage.
- Neutral mediation and European cooperation are viable late-game identities.
- Atlantic security alignment requires readiness, sovereignty safeguards, and a Northern settlement or lawful deferment.

## Celtic and Atlantic connections

Documented cultural connections link Ireland with Scotland, Wales, the Isle of Man, Brittany, and other Celtic regions through language study, folklore, literature, organisations, migration, and scholarship. These links do not establish a single political nation or automatic territorial claim.

The design boundary is:

- `H`: cultural congresses, language exchange, university and folklore cooperation, shipping, diaspora, labour, and local-government contacts.
- `P`: an Atlantic small-state conference, maritime compact, independent Celtic cultural league, trade and communications body, or voluntary federation among independent governments after political consent.
- `A`: sacral reconquest, impossible provincial jurisdiction, High-King claims beyond Ireland, Otherworld passages, or coercive pan-Celtic dominion.

Every political expansion requires target consent, independence status, diplomatic preparation, military capacity where force is used, foreign reaction, and postwar administration. A cultural milestone cannot create a claim or core.

## Part 4 research conclusions

1. Ireland's central material problem is provision under external dependence. National Provision therefore keeps Food Security, Energy Continuity, Transport Serviceability, and Maritime Access as four visible components. Industrial output is derived from their condition and the country's productive assets.
2. The 1938 agreements improve trade and sovereignty while transferring a serious defence burden.
3. Protection, industrial licensing, state companies, and emergency administration can create capacity and can also create inefficiency, patronage, and coercion.
4. Compulsory tillage, rationing, transport control, and fuel substitution require county and network gameplay with partial success and resistance.
5. Shipping is essential to neutrality. Fleet capacity, crew, repairs, insurance, routes, rescue, and belligerent pressure must be modelled.
6. Rail and road systems are constrained by coal, petrol, equipment, labour, and local distribution. Infrastructure rewards need regional purpose.
7. National Readiness must combine command, equipment, mobilisation, supply and mobility, warning, maritime protection, and intelligence coordination. Severe Provision shortages cap prolonged mobilisation gains.
8. Coast watching, Air Defence Command, coastal artillery, Air Corps, Marine Service, intelligence, and censorship form one connected defence and neutrality system.
9. Neutrality is active statecraft. It requires law, incident response, public legitimacy, foreign relations, territorial control, and managed covert cooperation.
10. British, American, German, and Vatican leverage remain source-specific. Access and aid require obligation and anti-dependency safeguards.
11. Northern Ireland contains a government, security apparatus, local authorities, parties, labour, strategic industry, services, and divided communities. Every settlement must govern these systems.
12. The 1940 offer is a crisis bargaining opportunity. It cannot bypass Stormont, unionist resistance, British implementation, Irish readiness, or constitutional detail.
13. Belfast's wartime industry and the Belfast Blitz connect unity politics to labour, housing, civil defence, production, humanitarian aid, and foreign strategy.
14. Claims and control are not cores. Integration requires transition government, security, finance, services, rights, compliance, and time.
15. Postwar gameplay must demobilise forces, unwind or transform controls, rebuild infrastructure, resolve foreign relations, and choose a constitutional identity.
16. Celtic and Atlantic cooperation begins with real cultural, shipping, labour, and academic links. Political or impossible orders require explicit additional gates.
17. Every Part 2 and Part 3 government needs a distinct material state. Political victory does not supply factories, equipment, consent, recognition, or territory.

## Part 4 canonical architecture reconciliation

The final Part 4 material architecture translates this research into four Provision components and seven Readiness components.

| System | Canonical components | Research reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| National Provision | Food Security, Energy Continuity, Transport Serviceability, Maritime Access | agriculture, fuel, internal distribution, and overseas carriage fail through different institutions and cannot compensate freely for one another |
| National Readiness | Command and Training, Equipment Sufficiency, Mobilisation Depth, Supply and Mobility, Coastal and Air Warning, Maritime Protection, Intelligence Coordination | manpower, equipment, headquarters, logistics, observation, harbour control, and intelligence were distinct weaknesses and capabilities |
| Neutrality Integrity | one visible value with incident history and source-specific leverage | neutrality depended on coherent law, capacity, enforcement, public legitimacy, foreign conduct, and controlled exceptions |
| Northern Settlement Ledger | Unionist Consent, Nationalist Consent, Labour Cooperation, British Flexibility, Security Stability, Integration Capacity | the constitutional, communal, labour, security, British, and administrative questions cannot be represented by one unity score |

The Coast Watching Service source confirms a network of 83 Look Out Posts manned by the Local Defence Force after the 1938 Treaty Port handover left coastal waters weakly protected. The service observed aircraft, shipping, and ship-to-shore communication until its disbandment in October 1945. This supports a sector-based warning system with training, communications, weather, rescue, and security requirements rather than a passive intelligence bonus.

The 1980 Oireachtas review of Irish Shipping records a merchant fleet of 41,105 gross registered tons at the outbreak of war, mainly small vessels, the severe supply crisis by late 1940, incorporation of Irish Shipping on 21 March 1941, and the operation of fifteen vessels carrying essential commodities during the war years. This supports a tiered tonnage, crew, charter, repair, route, and loss system.

The 1946 census occupational reports provide direct 1936 comparisons and county tables for farmers and farm size. They support county variation and reject a uniform national agricultural model.

Northern Ireland War Memorial research records the rapid wartime expansion of engineering and shipbuilding, major growth at Harland and Wolff and Short and Harland, increased female employment, strong union growth, and repeated industrial disputes. This supports a Northern industrial integration system that requires workers, contracts, energy, management, security, and labour settlement after any constitutional transfer or military occupation.

## Part 5 source decisions

1. The 1937 plebiscite and election are one campaign period with separate legal results.
2. The 1938 agreements produce separate trade, finance, port transfer, inventory and defence events.
3. Emergency law is broad enough to support censorship, detention, supply and service controls. The package therefore requires review, abuse, narrowing and expiry states.

5. Irish Shipping begins as a crisis institution. Its postwar survival depends on debt, fleet, crew, ownership and commercial purpose.

7. The 1940 proposals are a multi-actor bargaining crisis. They do not transfer Northern Ireland by event.
8. Blacksod is a technical-cooperation chain. It does not make Ireland a public belligerent by itself.
9. The Republic of Ireland Act and its commencement remain separate lifecycle states.
10. United Nations admission lies beyond the core wartime period and works as a late-game diplomatic chain.
