# Ireland Gameplay Overhaul and Focus Tree

## Part 2: Constitutional Politics and the Adaptive National Settlement

Feature slug: `ireland_focus_tree`

Planning status: Part 2 of 7

This file is the accepted Part 2 source specification within the seven-part planning package. It contains no implementation code and no final localisation. Every focus, route, law, idea, decision, mission, event, and interface name is a working label unless the text identifies a historical proper name.

Historical classification markers:

- `H` means documented history
- `P` means plausible speculation grounded in period actors, institutions, disputes, and strategic conditions
- `A` means deliberate absurdity

Part 2 fully designs the constitutional political family. It does not complete the IRA, Republican Congress, O'Duffy, Ailtirí, corporate dictatorship, or hidden mythic route families. It defines the interfaces through which those later routes can emerge.

## Part 2 design result

Part 2 resolves the political questions left open by Part 1.

1. `The National Settlement` uses the native balance-of-power presentation.
2. A paired dynamic decision category provides elections, cabinet bargaining, stakeholder action, constitutional missions, and crises.
3. A custom scripted GUI is not required for this mechanic. It would add presentation cost without adding control or clarity.
4. The opening balance runs from unfinished revolutionary authority on the negative side to constitutional state authority on the positive side.
5. The mechanic begins at `+25`, giving the state an advantage without making its legitimacy complete.
6. The mechanic has seven visible stages, route-specific labels, controlled momentum, stage persistence, and crisis thresholds.
7. Social Consent, National Readiness, and source-specific External Leverage remain separate companion values.
8. Elections use parliamentary seats and electoral blocs instead of simple ideology popularity.
9. The 1937 constitutional plebiscite and the 1937 general election share one campaign period but produce separate results.
10. The presidency, Seanad, cabinet, parties, laws, and coalition agreements have active lifecycles.
11. Éamon de Valera remains the normal Fianna Fáil leader. Seán Lemass requires an earned succession.
12. W. T. Cosgrave remains the normal Fine Gael leader before 1944. Richard Mulcahy and John A. Costello require distinct succession or coalition conditions.
13. James Dillon requires a difficult parliamentary mandate for pro-Allied belligerency.
14. William Norton leads a parliamentary Labour route. Its later revolutionary bridge remains gated for Part 3.
15. Church influence is divided into linked authority, service, and mobilisation networks rather than treated as one political party.
16. Constitutional vocationalism can deepen the Seanad and Article 19 councils while preserving Dáil supremacy.
17. Constitutional failure can redirect a route toward emergency government, party split, radical pressure, security command, or corporate capture. Those transformations are explicit and never disguised as normal constitutional rewards.

## Part 2 research conclusions

The constitutional design in this part rests on documented institutions and political transitions that were still unsettled during the late 1930s and Emergency years.

### Elections and parliamentary arithmetic

`H`

- The opening 1936 Dáil is represented as a 153-seat chamber inherited from the 1933 election, with a majority threshold of 77.
- The 1937 election and constitutional plebiscite take place together but produce separate mandates.
- The 1937 Dáil has 138 seats. Fianna Fáil holds 69, Fine Gael holds 48, Labour holds 13, and government depends on Labour support.
- The 1938 election can produce the historical Fianna Fáil majority of 77 seats after a confidence dispute connected to civil-service arbitration.
- The 1943 election returns Fianna Fáil to minority government, strengthens Labour, and introduces Clann na Talmhan as a significant parliamentary bloc.
- The 1944 election can follow a transport or confidence defeat after Labour has divided into Labour and National Labour.

Design consequence:

- elections remain active during the Emergency
- parliamentary support must be negotiated and delivered through policy promises
- a referendum victory does not award a parliamentary majority
- a government can survive as a minority, coalition, confidence government, or fixed-term national cabinet

### Constitution, President, and Seanad

`H`

- The 1937 Constitution makes the Government responsible to Dáil Éireann and requires Dáil assent for participation in war.
- The President appoints the Taoiseach after Dáil nomination and can refuse dissolution to a Taoiseach who no longer retains a majority when an alternative government can be formed.
- Douglas Hyde is the historical first President and the consensus result strongly preferred by historical AI.
- The Seanad contains 11 Taoiseach nominees, 6 university members, and 43 vocational-panel members.
- Article 19 supplies a constitutional basis for functional councils without creating an automatic corporate state.
- Article 45 supplies social-policy direction while remaining non-justiciable in the historical settlement.

Design consequence:

- the presidency acts as a constitutional referee, not a parallel executive
- the Seanad can delay, revise, expose, and mediate, while Dáil supremacy remains intact on every Part 2 route
- a stronger vocational settlement must be enacted through legislation, representation, and ratification

### Emergency and public-order law

`H`

- The Offences Against the State Act 1939 creates a security-law framework concerning unlawful organisations, state offences, exceptional courts, and internment machinery.
- The First Amendment of the Constitution in 1939 permits emergency authority during a war in which Ireland is not a participant.
- The Emergency Powers Act 1939 permits extensive regulation of supplies, services, transport, communications, movement, property, censorship, search, arrest, and detention.
- These powers operate through law and administration. Their existence does not remove Dáil responsibility or make every government action legitimate.

Design consequence:

- ordinary policing, public-order law, special courts, emergency orders, censorship, and internment are separate escalations
- strong authority can preserve neutrality and supply while damaging consent, courts, minorities, Labour, and republican legitimacy
- every constitutional route requires review, narrowing, sunset, or postwar repeal content

### Leadership and party transition

`H`

- Éamon de Valera remains the normal Fianna Fáil leader throughout the period.
- Seán Lemass gains authority through Industry and Commerce and wartime supply administration, while an early succession remains plausible speculation.
- W. T. Cosgrave remains Fine Gael leader until 1944.
- Richard Mulcahy succeeds Cosgrave as party leader, while his Civil War record can make him unacceptable as coalition Taoiseach.
- John A. Costello supplies a legalist compromise government leader without requiring him to become party leader first.
- James Dillon publicly breaks with Fine Gael over neutrality in 1942.
- William Norton leads a parliamentary Labour Party that supports neutrality while contesting emergency excess and pursuing social reform.
- The 1944 Labour split grows from union rivalry, candidate control, James Larkin, anti-communist accusations, and conflict between party and industrial leadership.

Design consequence:

- leadership changes require party, Dáil, cabinet, date, and crisis conditions
- party leader and Taoiseach can be different people
- Dillon and Lemass cannot receive government through a single focus
- National Labour is a real splinter with seats, organisation, coalition behavior, and a later reunion path

### Church influence and vocational thought

`H`

Catholic influence operates through hierarchy, education, social services, parish organisation, lay associations, moral authority, and diplomatic relationships. These institutions have overlapping interests and do not act as one command. The 1937 settlement recognises Catholic social weight while retaining guarantees for other denominations and rejecting a formal established church.

Design consequence:

- Church power is divided into policy domains, domestic service relationships, public mobilisation, and Vatican leverage
- minority confidence remains a separate constitutional constraint
- constitutional vocationalism can remain democratic only while competitive elections, Dáil responsibility, labour freedom, courts, and minority safeguards survive

## Player experience in the constitutional campaign

The constitutional campaign asks the player to govern a small state whose institutions are real, whose sovereignty is still contested, and whose leaders disagree about how independence should be completed.

The player repeatedly chooses among four kinds of authority.

- electoral authority obtained through elections, referenda, parliamentary support, and coalition bargains
- administrative authority obtained through functioning departments, courts, policing, and supply delivery
- social authority obtained through unions, churches, rural organisations, local government, and minority confidence
- emergency authority obtained through security legislation, mobilisation, censorship, and wartime direction

Each source can support the others. Each can also damage the others.

A government that wins a referendum but fails to feed cities can lose Social Consent. A government that commands the Dáil but relies on internment can strengthen the state while reviving revolutionary grievance. A Labour cabinet can pass reform while losing control of union rivalry. A Fine Gael coalition can defend legality while being vetoed by Civil War memory. A pro-Allied government can obtain foreign equipment while becoming dependent on British or American leverage.

The constitutional routes succeed when they create a state that can govern, defend itself, survive foreign pressure, manage Northern Ireland policy, and end exceptional powers without abandoning its route identity.

## The National Settlement

Working mechanic label: `The National Settlement`

### Mechanical purpose

The National Settlement measures which authority has the accepted right and practical capacity to define the state.

It is not a generic democracy meter. It is not identical to stability. It does not represent ideology popularity. It tracks the relationship between lawful state authority and the rival claims that the independence struggle, Civil War, partition, labour conflict, social institutions, or emergency conditions remain unfinished.

At the opening of the campaign:

- the positive side represents constitutional state authority
- the negative side represents the unfinished revolutionary mandate

After a route commitment, the same underlying value receives route-specific labels and crisis logic.

### Presentation decision

The mechanic uses three native surfaces.

1. The balance-of-power bar displays current position, current stage, route-specific pole labels, and major active contributors.
2. A dynamic decision category displays the actions, missions, elections, negotiations, and crises that can move the settlement.
3. National spirit and law tooltips display durable institutional consequences without duplicating the bar.

The design does not call for a separate scripted GUI.

Reasons:

- the native bar already provides the most important spatial information
- the decision category already supports costs, timers, mission objectives, and conditional visibility
- a second full interface would divide information and create duplicate controls
- the mechanic needs active gameplay depth rather than ornamental animation

Part 6 may plan a static seal family and stage icons for the category. Animation is not a requirement for the constitutional mechanic.

### Scale and opening state

The underlying scale is `-100` to `+100`.

- negative values favour the challenger or extra-state pole
- positive values favour the constitutional or governing pole
- zero represents a contested settlement rather than safety

Opening value on 1 January 1936: `+25`

Opening interpretation:

- the government controls the Dáil, civil service, courts, Garda, and Defence Forces
- Fianna Fáil has converted revolutionary legitimacy into government authority
- the IRA and uncompromising republican networks retain organisation and symbolic legitimacy
- partition and Civil War memory prevent the state from claiming complete national settlement
- the 1937 constitutional project has not yet given the state a renewed popular mandate

### Seven opening stages

All stage names are working labels.

| Range | Working stage label | Gameplay condition | Route access and danger |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `-100` to `-76` | `Revolutionary Command` | State institutions no longer monopolise political obedience. Armed or extra-parliamentary authority directs policy. | Constitutional focuses suspend. Part 3 revolutionary takeover gates become eligible if their other conditions exist. |
| `-75` to `-46` | `Armed Dual Authority` | Government and organised challengers compete for compliance. Security forces, local government, and political organisations may split. | Cabinet and military obedience crises intensify. Radical route recruitment and foreign contact become easier. |
| `-45` to `-16` | `Revolutionary Ascendancy` | Extra-state legitimacy shapes government choices even though the Dáil still functions. | Amnesty, negotiation, or crackdown actions become urgent. Some constitutional focuses require recovery above this band. |
| `-15` to `+15` | `Negotiated Settlement` | Neither pole can define the state alone. Compromise is possible and paralysis is common. | Coalition, amnesty, constitutional convention, and social compact routes gain access. Prolonged inaction creates drift through crisis momentum. |
| `+16` to `+45` | `Constitutional Ascendancy` | The state governs with contested but credible legitimacy. | Normal historical opening band. Elections, constitutional drafting, and institutional reforms are available. |
| `+46` to `+75` | `Executive Consolidation` | Government authority is strong enough to act decisively and risks outrunning consent. | Emergency law and central reform become easier. Repression, censorship, and cabinet dominance can create backlash. |
| `+76` to `+100` | `State Monopoly` | The state has displaced alternative authority and may have concentrated power beyond ordinary accountability. | Strong administrative rewards coexist with authoritarian failure risk. Constitutional routes must restore oversight or transform into an emergency regime. |

### Stage persistence and hysteresis

A stage change applies only after the value remains beyond the boundary for seven consecutive days.

After a stage change, the value must cross five points back into the previous band before the earlier stage can return.

Example:

- movement from `+45` to `+46` begins a seven-day confirmation period
- after confirmation, the stage becomes `Executive Consolidation`
- returning to `Constitutional Ascendancy` requires reaching `+40` or lower

This prevents a recurring event, daily modifier, or AI action from switching stage effects every few days.

### Settlement Momentum

The balance has no unconditional monthly drift.

Movement comes from choices, events, missions, law state, and unresolved pressure. A rolling working value called `Settlement Momentum` stores recent political direction.

Momentum range: `-5` to `+5`

Every thirty days:

- each positive momentum point moves the balance `+2`
- each negative momentum point moves the balance `-2`
- the maximum periodic movement is `10` points in either direction

Momentum sources:

- remain active for ninety days unless the source specifies a shorter crisis period
- appear in the mechanic breakdown
- are removed when their source is reversed, expires, or becomes irrelevant
- cannot stack from repeating the same action while its cooldown remains active

Direct balance changes are reserved for major outcomes such as a referendum, leadership transfer, military mutiny, government formation, attempted coup, successful amnesty, or emergency-law rupture.

Direct movement bands:

| Outcome weight | Direct movement |
| --- | --- |
| minor local or parliamentary result | `2` to `4` |
| substantial policy or stakeholder result | `5` to `8` |
| major election, law, or national crisis | `9` to `14` |
| regime-defining success or failure | `15` to `20` |

No single ordinary focus should move the balance by more than `8` without an attached election, event, or mission result.

### Opening momentum sources

| Source | Opening value | Duration or review | Explanation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Government control of the administration | `+2` | reviewed every 180 days | The state commands the ordinary machinery of government. |
| Fianna Fáil revolutionary inheritance | `-1` | removed or transformed by the constitutional settlement | The governing party still derives legitimacy from an unfinished republican claim. |
| Organised republican networks | `-1` | recalculated after security and amnesty outcomes | Extra-state republican organisation remains active. |
| Civil War institutional memory | `0` | activated by coercive or reconciliation choices | The opening memory is latent and becomes directional through policy. |

Opening net momentum is `-1`. This creates pressure to address the constitutional question without producing unavoidable collapse.

### Interaction rules among the values

The National Settlement measures authority. Social Consent measures willing compliance. National Readiness measures strategic capacity. External Leverage measures foreign constraint.

They do not substitute for each other.

Examples:

- high state authority with low Social Consent produces coercive government and route failure risk
- high Social Consent with low Dáil Mandate supports protest or reform but does not form a government
- high National Readiness with low Security Loyalty creates a dangerous armed institution
- high British Leverage can improve National Readiness while reducing freedom of action
- high Church influence can raise Social Consent for one policy and reduce Minority Confidence
- high Republican Network Strength can coexist with low public support if clandestine organisation remains intact

### Route commitment and balance transformation

A route commitment does not reset the mechanic.

When a constitutional route commits:

1. the current value is preserved
2. values outside `-60` to `+60` are clamped to the nearest boundary
3. obsolete opening momentum sources are removed
4. route-specific pole labels replace the opening labels
5. route-specific stakeholders become visible
6. route-specific crises and decisions replace opening constitutional actions
7. the route receives a six-month settlement review mission

The six-month review tests whether the new government has converted its route victory into working authority.

Common review requirements:

- valid government or confidence agreement
- Cabinet Cohesion at least `45`
- Dáil Mandate at least `45`
- Social Consent at least `40`
- no unresolved constitutional crisis

Success grants access to the route's institutional middle game. Partial success imposes a reform or coalition condition. Failure activates the route's first major instability branch.

### Route-stage effect principles

Each route uses the same seven numeric bands but receives route-specific stage descriptions and consequences.

Stage effects follow four rules.

1. The centre band provides flexibility and weak execution.
2. Moderate route-side dominance provides the route's strongest sustainable state.
3. Moderate challenger-side dominance opens compromise or corrective branches.
4. Extreme dominance on either side creates a failure or transformation risk.

No route receives unconditional positive bonuses for reaching an extreme.

### National Settlement crisis triggers

A settlement crisis can be triggered by one major condition or by three minor conditions lasting thirty days.

Major conditions:

- balance at `-76` or lower
- balance at `+76` or higher with Social Consent below `35`
- Cabinet Cohesion below `20`
- Dáil Mandate below `20`
- Security Loyalty below `20`
- government-formation deadline expires
- President refuses dissolution
- constitutional referendum fails
- war-entry vote fails after public commitment
- a route-specific leader is removed without a valid successor

Minor conditions:

- Social Consent below `30`
- Minority Confidence below `25`
- Republican Network Strength above `75`
- Labour Organisation above `75` during an unresolved strike
- Church Institutional Influence above `80` during a Church-state confrontation
- two foreign leverage sources above `60`
- emergency law at maximum breadth without legislative review
- Cabinet Cohesion below `35`
- Dáil Mandate below `35`

A crisis creates:

- one active timed mission
- two to four response decisions
- one initiating event
- one partial-success event
- one failure event
- one cleanup event or effect handoff

Part 5 will map final event identifiers and complete option wording direction. Part 2 defines the political outcomes.

### Settlement cleanup rules

Every crisis and route transition must remove or transform:

- obsolete momentum sources
- expired stakeholder modifiers
- election campaign actions
- government-formation targets
- defeated candidate flags
- invalid coalition agreements
- old leader contest missions
- prior route decision categories
- emergency actions no longer legal under the current law state

A failed constitutional route can leave durable memory through one transformed idea or stakeholder value. It must not leave stale missions or duplicate route controls.

## Parliamentary and constitutional system

### Parliamentary seat model

The system tracks party seats separately from ideology popularity.

Opening 1936 seat ledger derived from the 1933 election:

| Bloc | Formal seats | Routine confidence votes | Design note |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Fianna Fáil | `77` | `76` | The documented party total includes the outgoing Ceann Comhairle. His seat remains a Fianna Fáil seat in the ledger, while the office is held outside routine party voting. |
| Fine Gael parliamentary inheritance | `59` | `59` | Combines the documented `48` Cumann na nGaedheal seats and `11` National Centre Party seats after the formation of Fine Gael. This is a `P` continuity abstraction for the 1936 start. |
| Labour | `8` | `8` | Documented 1933 party total. |
| Independent and minor deputies | `9` | `9` | One Independent Labour deputy and eight other independents form the opening non-party ledger. |
| Total | `153` | `152` | Formal seats always sum to the sitting Dáil. Routine voting strength excludes the Ceann Comhairle reserve. |
| Majority benchmark | `77` | division-specific | Formal government arithmetic uses `77` seats as the opening benchmark. Actual divisions use members present and voting, with a tied division handled through the Ceann Comhairle reserve. |

`Ceann Comhairle Reserve` is a parliamentary status rather than a party bonus. It cannot be traded in coalition negotiations, counted as an ordinary confidence vote, moved by an election focus, or duplicated after a change of office. A tied division creates a short constitutional event that applies established continuity and further-consideration principles according to the type of vote.

After the 1937 election the Dáil uses:

- `138` total seats
- `70` seats for an outright majority

Seat counts change through elections, defections, by-elections, party splits, and coalition realignment. Ordinary focus completion does not directly award seats.

| Border and northern-facing counties | partition, security, smuggling, military risk, northern contacts | Fianna Fáil, Fine Gael security, Dillon, radical republican pressure |

Each bloc can produce:

- a `3` to `6` seat swing for a clear winner
- a `1` to `2` seat swing for a narrow winner
- no swing for a draw

The final election result also applies national corrections for:

- Social Consent
- Dáil Mandate
- Cabinet Cohesion
- shortages and unemployment
- National Readiness during wartime
- party unity or split
- constitutional campaign outcome
- leader credibility
- coalition agreement violations

The model does not require simulated constituency counts. It must still explain each seat swing through named blocs and national factors.

### Election campaign phases

Every scheduled or crisis election has four phases.

#### Phase 1: proclamation and party position

Duration: 14 days

Actions:

- confirm leader
- confirm party platform family
- decide coalition signals
- select priority electoral blocs
- resolve disqualified or splinter candidates

Failure effects:

- candidate confusion
- party split pressure
- reduced campaign action capacity

#### Phase 2: regional campaign

Duration: 35 to 49 days depending on election type

The player receives a limited number of campaign operations. Operations use varied costs such as civilian-factory burden, trains, fuel, party funds represented by political power only where fitting, local organisation, or temporary minister unavailability.

Campaign operations include:

- urban price and housing programme
- rural market and credit tour
- border sovereignty campaign
- labour congress and workplace campaign
- constitutional and minority guarantee campaign
- defence and neutrality campaign

Each operation targets one electoral bloc and changes one or more stakeholder values. Repeating the same operation in the same bloc has diminishing returns.

#### Phase 3: closing crisis

Duration: 7 days

One context-sensitive event tests the campaign's weakest issue.

Possible issues:

- food or fuel shortage
- clerical intervention
- union dispute
- security incident
- foreign access allegation
- candidate defection
- constitutional wording controversy
- military readiness disclosure

The closing crisis can create a final seat shift of `0` to `4` seats.

#### Phase 4: result and government formation

The election event records:

- party seats
- electoral-bloc winners
- leader result
- Dáil Mandate
- government formation eligibility
- party split or retirement triggers

A government is not created until the formation sequence completes.

### Scheduled and conditional elections

| Election | Historical condition | Alternate conditions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1937 election | Shared constitutional campaign and Dáil reduction | May become a constitutional-crisis election if the draft is withdrawn or the government falls before polling. |

| 1943 election | Wartime election after the government fails to secure support for extension | An extension can pass only with broad opposition consent and high constitutional legitimacy. Refusal or abuse produces election pressure. |

### 1937 constitutional campaign

The constitutional campaign combines one shared preparation period with two separate results.

#### Draft components

The government must settle six constitutional components.

1. executive and cabinet authority
2. presidency and dissolution powers
3. Seanad and vocational representation
4. religion, minority guarantees, and education
5. language and national identity
6. territory, sovereignty, and war powers

Each component offers two or three policy directions. Choices affect stakeholders and later route access.

Examples of policy direction, all working labels:

| Component | Direction | Classification | Main effects |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Executive | `Strong Responsible Cabinet` | `H` | Raises Administrative Confidence and executive capacity while increasing overreach risk. |
| Executive | `Parliamentary Safeguards` | `P` | Raises Dáil Mandate and coalition viability while reducing emergency speed. |
| Presidency | `Constitutional Referee` | `H` | Supports Hyde agreement and later Article 26 or dissolution crises. |
| Presidency | `Partisan National Presidency` | `P` | Raises governing-party control and creates a contested first election. |
| Seanad | `Revisionary Vocational House` | `H` | Creates the historical panel structure with constrained independence. |
| Seanad | `Independent Social Chamber` | `P` | Raises vocational influence and later constitutional-vocational access. |
| Religion | `Special Position with Guarantees` | `H` | Raises Church influence while preserving named minority protections. |
| Religion | `Equal Constitutional Recognition` | `P` | Raises Minority Confidence and creates Church negotiation costs. |
| Language | `State-Led Gaelic Commitment` | `H` | Supports serious cultural policy and later hidden-route prerequisites without revealing them. |

| Territory | `All-Island National Claim` | `H` | Preserves the constitutional territorial claim and partition pressure. |

#### Campaign support pools

The plebiscite uses support pools from:

- Fianna Fáil organisation
- Fine Gael opposition or conditional support
- Labour support, neutrality, or opposition
- Church hierarchy and parish mobilisation
- minority denominations and civic organisations
- rural local government
- republican veterans and networks

Support is not identical to party endorsement. Fine Gael voters can support individual constitutional provisions while opposing the government. Labour can support the state framework while campaigning against social clauses.

#### Plebiscite result bands

| Approval share | Working result | Consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `60` percent or higher | `Strong Popular Settlement` | Direct National Settlement movement toward the state, strong Dáil Mandate, and reduced early constitutional crisis risk. Concessions used to reach this result remain binding. |
| `55` to `59.9` percent | `Accepted Settlement` | Historical-quality mandate. The Constitution enters force with an organised opposition and normal future review pressure. |
| `50` to `54.9` percent | `Narrow Settlement` | Constitution passes with a legitimacy vulnerability. Opposition, Church, Labour, or republican objections create route-specific review missions. |
| below `50` percent | `Rejected Draft` | Constitution fails. The government enters a revision, resignation, or election crisis. The Free State framework continues temporarily. |

Historical AI aims for `56` to `58` percent approval.

#### Separate election result

The constitutional approval share does not set the Dáil seat result directly.

It modifies:

- Dáil Mandate
- Fianna Fáil seat swing
- Fine Gael mobilisation
- Labour bargaining power
- non-party constitutional candidates

A passed Constitution with a Fianna Fáil minority is a valid and historically grounded outcome.

### Government formation

Government formation begins after every election or loss of majority.

Normal negotiation duration: 30 days

Hard deadline: 45 days

Formation rules:

| Largest party seats | Formation route |
| --- | --- |
| `70` or more | May form a single-party majority government. A coalition remains possible if chosen for national emergency reasons. |
| `65` to `69` | May form a minority government with a confidence-and-supply agreement. |
| `55` to `64` | May lead a coalition if partners bring the total to at least `70`. |
| below `55` | Requires a broad coalition, national government, or another party leader able to assemble `70` seats. |

The largest party receives the first formation attempt unless a prior alliance has an announced majority.

Formation resources:

- cabinet portfolios
- legislative programme commitments
- law-state guarantees
- emergency oversight
- labour arbitration commitments
- rural and trade policy commitments
- Northern Ireland consultation
- leadership compromise

Formation costs are political commitments rather than a flat political-power payment.

### Confidence and supply

A confidence-and-supply agreement contains three to five clauses.

Potential clauses:

- support on confidence motions
- support on annual supply
- arbitration legislation
- price and wage review
- constitutional amendment review
- emergency powers sunset review
- housing or rural works programme
- neutrality commitment
- no conscription commitment

Each clause has:

- a sponsoring party
- a completion deadline
- a visible status
- a breach consequence

Breaking one clause reduces Coalition Cohesion or Dáil Mandate. Breaking two active clauses allows the supporting party to withdraw confidence through event and decision logic.

### Coalition Cohesion

Coalition Cohesion runs from `0` to `100` when more than one party enters government.

Starting cohesion is calculated from:

- ideological and policy distance
- Civil War memory
- leader compatibility
- number of coalition partners
- clarity of the programme for government
- allocation of senior portfolios
- emergency or war pressure
- prior broken agreements

Working bands:

| Range | Working label | Consequence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `0` to `19` | `Collapsed Agreement` | Government formation fails or cabinet resigns. |
| `20` to `39` | `Cabinet Hostage` | Every major bill risks partner veto. |
| `40` to `59` | `Transactional Coalition` | Government functions through continuing bargains. |
| `60` to `79` | `Working Coalition` | Programme can advance with manageable disputes. |
| `80` to `100` | `National Compact` | Coalition can sustain major reform or wartime policy. Dominant partners may still resent loss of identity. |

### Cabinet system

The cabinet is represented through:

- leader
- finance portfolio
- industry and supplies portfolio
- justice and security portfolio
- defence portfolio
- external affairs portfolio
- social or labour portfolio where the route creates one

Each route identifies preferred ministers and compromise candidates in the constitutional politics matrix.

Cabinet Cohesion changes when:

- portfolios match route competence
- ministers complete missions
- cabinet factions receive or lose influence
- a minister resigns over neutrality, emergency law, Church policy, or labour policy
- the leader overrides collective responsibility

A cabinet reshuffle is a structured decision family with a cooldown. It can solve competence or faction problems while reducing immediate cohesion.

### Presidency system

The presidency becomes active after the 1937 constitutional settlement.

Historical first presidency:

- Douglas Hyde is selected through inter-party agreement
- no contested poll occurs
- Church, minority, cultural, and party stakeholders receive a unity outcome

Contested first presidency, `P`:

- becomes available if the major-party agreement fails
- uses a short nomination phase
- allows Hyde, Seán T. O'Kelly, and Seán Mac Eoin subject to route conditions
- produces a separate legitimacy effect from the Dáil election

Presidential powers represented in gameplay:

1. appointment of a Taoiseach after Dáil nomination
2. refusal of dissolution when the Taoiseach has lost a majority
3. Article 26 referral of a bill to the Supreme Court
4. Council of State consultation during constitutional crisis
5. formal role in emergency proclamations and national ceremonies

The President does not direct ordinary policy.

#### Presidential dissolution crisis

Trigger:

- Taoiseach loses a confidence vote
- Taoiseach requests dissolution
- another leader appears able to command a majority
- President has sufficient independence from the government

Outcomes:

- dissolution granted and election begins
- dissolution refused and alternative government formation begins
- referral to consultation produces a delayed decision
- constitutional confrontation damages both offices and opens an emergency-government failure path

### Seanad system

The Seanad is activated after the Constitution enters force.

Historical composition:

- 11 Taoiseach nominees
- 6 university members
- 43 vocational panel members

Panel seat distribution:

- cultural and educational panel: 5
- agricultural panel: 11
- labour panel: 11
- industrial and commercial panel: 9
- administrative panel: 7

Seanad Influence is not a fourth national bar. It is calculated from:

- independence of the panels
- government nominations
- party control of the electorate
- university opposition
- stakeholder mobilisation
- Article 19 councils

Seanad actions can:

- delay or revise ordinary legislation
- force a public political event
- improve a bill and raise Administrative Confidence
- expose government capture and reduce Dáil Mandate
- mediate labour, education, or rural disputes

The Seanad cannot permanently block a government with a valid Dáil majority under the constitutional route.

### Constitutional electoral rule

All Part 2 constitutional routes retain proportional representation by the single transferable vote and competitive Dáil elections.

A route that crosses those boundaries has entered a Part 3 authoritarian or revolutionary transformation.
# Fianna Fáil and constitutional republican government

## Fianna Fáil shared route identity

Classification: `H` for Fianna Fáil government, de Valera, Lemass, constitutional drafting, sovereignty policy, the 1938 settlement, neutrality, and Emergency administration. `P` for alternate leadership timing, revised constitutional packages, and non-historical coalition outcomes.

The Fianna Fáil family asks whether a party formed to reject the Treaty settlement can become the durable constitutional state without surrendering the republican claim that gave it legitimacy.

Its advantages are:

- incumbency and administrative control
- a national party organisation
- republican prestige
- leadership authority around de Valera
- control of the constitutional timetable
- the ability to join sovereignty, trade, neutrality, and state-building into one programme

Its vulnerabilities are:

- dependence on de Valera
- tension between veterans, administrators, younger organisers, farmers, workers, and local branches
- IRA claims that constitutional government has abandoned the revolution
- Labour leverage during minority government
- Church and minority disputes over the Constitution
- a weak defence establishment
- the danger that Emergency administration becomes permanent executive rule

## Route entry and constitutional lock

Fianna Fáil can enter its full constitutional route through any of these outcomes.

1. Win or lead government after the 1937 election and pass the Constitution.
2. Pass the Constitution while governing through a written Labour or independent confidence agreement.
3. Recover from a rejected draft through a revised Constitution and a valid Dáil mandate.
4. Lead a temporary national cabinet during a severe emergency, then secure a Dáil vote and a fixed review schedule.

Party popularity alone never locks the route.

The route lock requires:

- a recognised Taoiseach and cabinet
- Dáil Mandate at least `45`
- Cabinet Cohesion at least `45`
- Social Consent at least `40`
- National Settlement above `-46`
- no unresolved government-formation mission

## Fianna Fáil internal blocs

These blocs are contextual stakeholder groups. They do not become extra permanent national meters.

| Bloc | Historical or plausible basis | Main demand | Main risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| de Valera leadership circle | `H` | constitutional control, sovereignty, disciplined neutrality | succession paralysis and party-state fusion |
| Lemass developmental group | `H` | industry, supply administration, departmental delivery | technocratic overreach and rural alienation |
| Aiken sovereignty and defence group | `H` | external independence, security, republican credibility | security centralisation and alignment disputes |
| MacEntee fiscal and administrative group | `H` | revenue, protection, administrative discipline | austerity, business conflict, and cabinet rivalry |
| O'Kelly organisation and ceremonial group | `H` | party loyalty, republican symbolism, institutional appointments | partisan presidency and patronage |
| old republican veterans | `H` | recognition, pensions, partition commitment, dignity | extra-state loyalty and amnesty capture |
| county and rural organisers | `H` | prices, local works, candidate autonomy, agricultural protection | resistance to central candidate control |
| urban and younger organisers | `P` as an organised succession bloc | jobs, housing, industry, party renewal | conflict with older local machines |

A cabinet cannot integrate every bloc at maximum influence. Appointments and programme choices create visible winners and disappointed groups.

## Fianna Fáil route transformation

Positive pole working label: `Constitutional Republican Government`

Negative pole working label: `Unfinished Republican Claim`

The route normally inherits the opening National Settlement position.

On a valid route lock:

- remove `Pending Constitutional Mandate`
- convert `Fianna Fáil Revolutionary Inheritance` into a route-specific source
- add direct movement of `+4` after an accepted Constitution
- add direct movement of `+2` after a valid minority or coalition agreement
- withhold the movement if the government bypasses Dáil formation or intimidates the plebiscite

The six-month review asks whether Fianna Fáil has converted constitutional victory into working government.

Review requirements:

- Dáil Mandate at least `50`
- Cabinet Cohesion at least `50`
- Social Consent at least `45`
- one functioning relationship with Labour, rural organisations, minority bodies, or lawful republican veterans
- no unresolved referendum, security, or presidential crisis

## De Valera route stances

The de Valera government contains three compatible but competing stances. The player can combine two. Attempting to maximise all three creates contradictory promises and Cabinet Cohesion loss.

### Constitutional stewardship

Working route label: `The Architect's Republic`

Mechanical identity:

- govern through Dáil confidence and constitutional procedure
- preserve Hyde as a consensus President
- maintain active neutrality and source-balanced diplomacy
- use selective emergency authority with scheduled review
- keep the National Settlement in `Constitutional Ascendancy` or the safe part of `Executive Consolidation`

Distinct gameplay:

- Council of State and Article 26 review events
- parliamentary renewal missions
- constitutional appointments
- public mandate and press-confidence decisions
- diplomatic autonomy clauses

Primary tradeoff:

- careful legal process limits rapid mobilisation and supply direction
- broad consultation gives Labour, Fine Gael, Church, and minority actors leverage over later laws

### Republican reconciliation

Working route label: `Bring the Revolution Home`

Mechanical identity:

- move the National Settlement toward the centre without allowing armed dual authority
- convert veterans and lawful republican organisations into constitutional participants
- use conditional release, service, pensions, local boards, and anti-partition consultation
- require weapons compliance and one recognised chain of authority

Core missions:

1. `Review the Prison Lists`
2. `Register Lawful Republican Associations`
3. `County Reconciliation Boards`
4. `Arms, Oaths, and Public Service`
5. `Veterans in Civil Defence`

Concrete costs:

- administrative capacity
- pension and relief expenditure
- temporary Garda or military deployment
- political concessions on commemorations and partition policy
- risk that released organisers strengthen active networks

Partial success:

- veterans enter lawful politics while IRA networks remain organised
- Social Consent rises, while security pressure and Northern expectations persist

Failure:

- amnesty participants return to armed organisation
- Garda or Defence Forces officers resist implementation
- pro-Treaty communities and victims mobilise against the settlement
- the route enters `Republican Legitimacy Collapse`

### Emergency direction

Working route label: `Government During the Emergency`

Mechanical identity:

- use emergency orders, censorship, detention, supply controls, and departmental coordination
- move the National Settlement toward `Executive Consolidation`
- convert administrative capacity into National Readiness
- accept a continuing Social Consent and review burden

Core missions:

1. `Coordinate the Departments`
2. `Secure Essential Supplies`
3. `Review Censorship Orders`
4. `Charge, Release, or Review Detainees`
5. `Renew the Powers in the Dáil`
6. `Set the Postwar Sunset`

Emergency authority is not a free strength route.

- every renewal consumes Dáil Mandate or requires a coalition concession
- detention without a visible security threat increases Republican Network Strength
- delegated censorship can damage Minority Confidence and Labour unity
- broad supply control can improve delivery while weakening Cabinet Cohesion through ministerial rivalry

## De Valera failure states

### `Republic Without a Mandate`

The Constitution is rejected, Dáil Mandate falls below `35`, and the government cannot pass a revised draft or win a new election.

Consequences:

- constitutional route lock is suspended
- opposition formation and revised convention open
- Republican Network Strength rises
- de Valera may resign, seek a new mandate, or accept a national cabinet

### `Party and State Become One`

National Settlement reaches `State Monopoly`, Cabinet Cohesion remains high, and Dáil Mandate or Social Consent falls below `35`.

Consequences:

- party appointments replace neutral administration
- opposition, courts, Labour, and minority actors coordinate restoration pressure
- the route can restore oversight or hand an emergency-state condition to Part 3

### `The Revolution Outside the Door`

Republican Network Strength exceeds `75`, reconciliation fails, and Security Loyalty falls below `40`.

Consequences:

- dual-authority missions begin
- ordinary capstones suspend
- Part 3 receives a revolutionary access state only after the mapped failure mission

### `Emergency Without an End`

Full Emergency Powers continue after the external emergency while review promises are broken.

Consequences:

- Indefinite Emergency Rule activates
- Social Consent and Dáil Mandate decline
- permanent-security and constitutional-restoration branches compete

### `Succession Deferred`

De Valera remains indispensable after repeated electoral, health, cabinet, or wartime succession warnings.

Consequences:

- younger organisers and Lemass standing become hostile rather than cooperative
- cabinet reshuffles lose effectiveness
- an eventual transition begins under crisis pressure

# Seán Lemass and developmental succession

## Lemass historical and speculative boundary

`H`

Seán Lemass serves as Minister for Industry and Commerce from 1932 to 1939, becomes Minister for Supplies during the Emergency, and builds authority through industrial, commercial, procurement, and supply administration.

`P`

An early Lemass succession is possible only after he has built a cabinet, party, parliamentary, and social coalition. Completing industrial focuses alone never removes de Valera.

Historical AI does not complete an early succession before the end of the Emergency.

## Developmental Standing

Lemass uses a contextual standing from `0` to `6`. It appears only during Fianna Fáil leadership and succession content.

Standing rises from:

1. successful trade and industrial preparation
2. constitutional drafting competence
3. urban and technical party support
4. a completed Labour, rural, or Church social bargain
5. successful wartime supply administration
6. de Valera endorsement or an accepted party-conference result

Standing falls from:

- severe shortage or procurement failure
- rural price collapse
- coercive wage policy without settlement
- public association with foreign dependency
- Church-service conflict without replacement capacity
- an attempted leadership challenge before the constitutional settlement

Standing effects:

| Standing | Meaning | Route effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `0` to `1` | departmental subordinate | no leadership route, limited economic adviser role |
| `2` to `3` | recognised moderniser | cabinet and party-reform decisions open |
| `4` | credible successor | succession negotiations become possible |
| `5` | leading successor | party and Dáil transition can succeed under normal conditions |
| `6` | national developmental mandate | succession receives a strong starting coalition, with technocratic backlash risk |

## Earliest and normal succession windows

An early succession normally cannot begin before completion of the 1938 sovereignty settlement.

It may begin earlier only when:

- the 1937 Constitution is in force
- de Valera voluntarily accepts the presidency or publicly designates a successor
- Lemass Standing is at least `4`
- Cabinet Cohesion and Dáil Mandate are each at least `50`

Normal accelerated succession becomes available after one of these conditions.

1. Lemass successfully administers wartime supplies for at least one major crisis cycle.
2. Fianna Fáil loses a majority or election because voters demand administrative renewal.
3. de Valera retires after completing the constitutional and sovereignty settlements.
4. a cabinet crisis produces a lawful party leadership ballot that Lemass wins.
5. de Valera becomes President through the constitutional route.

A failed challenge leaves Lemass in cabinet, weakens his standing, and strengthens either de Valera loyalists or another opposition route. It does not automatically create civil war.

## Succession process

The succession is a three-stage political mission.

### Stage 1: cabinet compact

Duration: 45 days

Objectives:

- secure at least three major portfolios or cabinet blocs
- preserve one de Valera loyalist guarantee
- select a rural, Labour, Church, or veteran reassurance package
- keep Cabinet Cohesion above `45`

### Stage 2: party conference and county organisation

Duration: 60 days

Objectives:

- win younger and urban organisers
- prevent county branches from running splinter candidates
- obtain de Valera endorsement, neutrality, or a valid contested result
- keep Party Unity above `50`

### Stage 3: Dáil nomination

Duration: 30 days

Objectives:

- secure Fianna Fáil seats and required coalition support
- allocate portfolios
- confirm constitutional continuity
- avoid a presidential or government-formation crisis

Full success installs Lemass with a working mandate. Partial success creates a dependent coalition or party truce. Failure leaves a caretaker, a rival leader claim, or a Fine Gael and Labour formation opportunity.

## Lemass route transformation

Positive pole working label: `Developmental State Capacity`

Negative pole working label: `Traditional and Revolutionary Constraint`

On valid succession:

- preserve the current National Settlement value
- cap inherited values above `+60` or below `-60`
- remove de Valera-specific leadership momentum
- add `+4` direct movement for a consensual succession
- add no direct movement for a contested succession
- apply a six-month review testing whether delivery has replaced personal leadership

The challenger pole contains democratic and non-democratic opposition. Rural bodies, Labour, Church services, veterans, local branches, and de Valera loyalists can all resist a developmental executive for different reasons.

# William Norton and parliamentary Labour

## Norton route identity

Labour begins as a constitutional, trade-union-linked party that broadly supports neutrality and can sustain or replace a minority government. It is divided by organisational rivalry, anti-communist accusation, religious pressure, union affiliation, candidate selection, and the relationship between parliamentary leadership and industrial organisation.

The route's strongest outcome is a parliamentary social republic. A workers' republic formed through revolutionary cooperation remains a Part 3 bridge.

## Norton route entry

Labour may enter government through:

- a support agreement with Fianna Fáil after 1937
- a coalition with Fine Gael or a Costello cabinet
- a Labour-led coalition after a strong 1943-style election
- an outright or near-majority election result in a major alternate campaign
- a national emergency coalition

Norton becomes Taoiseach only after a Dáil nomination. In a coalition led by another party he may serve as Tánaiste, social-policy minister, or labour leader outside cabinet according to the charter.

## Norton route transformation

The National Settlement changes to `Parliamentary Social Mandate` against `Extra-Parliamentary Labour Power`.

## Labour Movement Unity

`Labour Movement Unity` is a temporary internal value from 0 to 100. It begins at `58`.

| ---: | --- | --- |
| 75 to 100 | `Integrated Labour Movement` | party and major affiliates coordinate while retaining internal votes |
| 60 to 74 | `Working Unity` | manageable rivalry and shared election work |
| 45 to 59 | `Factional Labour` | recurring candidate and policy disputes |
| 25 to 44 | `Imminent Split` | affiliates threaten withdrawal and separate candidates |
| 0 to 24 | `Divided Labour` | National Labour or another breakaway becomes active |

Unity changes through union conferences, candidate selection, strike policy, anti-communist disputes, relations with James Larkin and William O'Brien, Church pressure, coalition compromises, and the handling of emergency wage controls.

## Norton internal route structure

### `Labour as the Parliamentary Conscience`

Develops scrutiny of emergency law, workers' living standards, detention, unemployment, and neutrality from the opposition benches.

### `Support Without Surrender`

Creates a formal Fianna Fáil support agreement with enforceable social clauses. Labour gains policy and risks electoral absorption.

### `A Coalition of Constitutional Reform`

Creates a Fine Gael, Costello, farmers, and Labour agreement. Labour gains ministries and must manage Civil War and fiscal disputes.

### `Labour to Government`

Builds a Labour-led electoral campaign, cabinet slate, and rural as well as urban programme.

### `Wages, Prices, and the Right to Organise`

Creates arbitration, wage, strike, union-recognition, and emergency-control missions.

### `Homes, Work, and Social Insurance`

Creates public works, housing, unemployment, pension, health, and family-support commitments. Part 4 maps economic projects.

### `Rural Labour and the Small Farmer`

Prevents the route from becoming a Dublin-only programme. It links agricultural wages, smallholders, land, roads, local authorities, and Clann na Talmhan.

### `Neutrality and the Defence Council`

Supports strict neutrality and defence while preserving the possibility of an anti-fascist intervention debate if campaign conditions change.

### `The Unity of Labour`

Resolves candidate selection, affiliate status, Larkin, O'Brien, anti-communist accusations, and the 1944 split.

### `Parliamentary Social Republic`

Completes the constitutional social settlement and locks out the full revolutionary route unless a later catastrophic failure reopens it.

## Coalition variants

### Fianna Fáil and Labour

Labour requires social spending, wage arbitration, union rights, and emergency-law review. Fianna Fáil retains the Taoiseach and sovereignty programme unless Labour becomes the larger partner.

This coalition is strong on neutrality and republican state legitimacy. It is vulnerable to Labour absorption and rural budget disputes.

### Fine Gael and Labour

Labour requires social investment and rights guarantees. Fine Gael requires legal procedure and fiscal controls.

This coalition can produce a broad constitutional reform government. It is vulnerable to Civil War memory, military policy, and spending conflict.

### Labour and Clann na Talmhan

This coalition requires rural wages, smallholder prices, local roads, land policy, and decentralised public works.

It can make Labour nationally competitive. It is vulnerable to food-price, taxation, and Church-service disputes.

### Labour-led broad coalition

A Labour Taoiseach requires Labour to hold at least 35 seats, be the largest coalition party, and secure at least 70 votes through partners. The route then chooses a social-democratic majority programme or a looser national social compact.

## Labour unity crisis of 1943 to 1944

The crisis begins when:

- James Larkin seeks a Labour candidacy or parliamentary role
- the ITGWU and Labour executive dispute candidate authority
- Unity is below 65
- anti-communist accusations have not been resolved

The player must complete three missions.

1. `Settle Candidate Authority` defines whether local branches, party executive, or affiliated unions control nomination.
2. `Guarantee Union Autonomy` protects affiliates without giving one union control of the party.
3. `Answer the Red Scare` uses a constitutional, religious, labour, and civil-liberties response rather than repression or empty denial.

Outcomes:

- Unity at 70 or more prevents the split. `[P]`
- Unity from 45 to 69 creates an autonomous affiliate caucus and seat penalties. `[P]`
- Unity below 45 creates National Labour with its own parliamentary strength, advisers, events, and coalition red lines. `[H/P]`

# Church influence and constitutional vocational politics

## Design principle

The domestic Catholic Church is a set of institutions and networks, not one lever owned by the Vatican and not one political party.

The feature separates:

1. episcopal and clerical authority
2. education, hospital, welfare, and charitable service institutions
3. lay devotional and community organisations
4. Catholic social and vocational thinkers
5. Vatican diplomatic leverage

These actors often cooperate. They can also disagree over methods, appointments, party alliances, local priorities, and the acceptable reach of the state.

- State authority rises under inspection and public standards. It can fall if administration lacks the capacity to replace existing services.
- Vatican leverage changes diplomatic access and foreign reaction. It does not directly set domestic domain states.

## Constitutional crisis architecture

Constitutional routes require failure states that change play. A crisis is not resolved by paying political power to remove a modifier. Each crisis creates a political deadline, concrete objectives, stakeholder movement, a partial result, and cleanup.

### Crisis 1: rejected constitutional draft

Classification: `P` outcome from an `H` plebiscite framework

Trigger:

- approval below 50 percent

Immediate effects:

- Constitution does not enter force
- Dáil Mandate falls
- Cabinet Cohesion is tested
- Free State framework remains temporarily
- presidency and new Seanad remain unavailable

Response routes:

1. revise the draft through a cross-party convention
2. resign and contest a constitutional election
3. retain government and introduce a narrower draft
4. attempt emergency enactment, creating an authoritarian failure gateway

Timed mission:

`Resolve the Constitutional Authority` as a working label

Duration: 120 days

Success:

- revised plebiscite or valid election mandate begins

Partial success:

- interim constitutional statute with mandatory later plebiscite

Failure:

- government loses authority
- National Settlement moves sharply toward challenger-side claims
- radical and corporate route gateways gain pressure

Cleanup:

- remove old draft choices that were explicitly rejected
- preserve only provisions carried into the revised draft
- clear campaign actions

### Crisis 2: government formation deadlock

Trigger:

- no leader obtains Dáil nomination within 30 days

Immediate effects:

- caretaker government
- Cabinet Cohesion cannot rise above `40`
- major route focuses suspend

Objectives:

- assemble 70 seats
- agree confidence and supply
- nominate a compromise Taoiseach
- call a valid election

Hard deadline: 45 days after election or resignation

Partial success:

- minority caretaker receives a 90-day confidence trial

Failure:

- election begins automatically where constitutionally valid
- if election is impossible under a larger crisis, a national emergency cabinet forms with low legitimacy and strict sunset

Cleanup:

- remove invalid coalition targets
- reset negotiation promises not included in final agreement
- retain public breaches as memory

### Crisis 3: no-confidence defeat and dissolution request

Trigger:

- government loses confidence motion

Immediate decision:

- resign and invite alternative formation
- request dissolution
- dispute whether the vote was confidence, creating court and presidential controversy

Objectives:

- prove alternative majority
- obtain presidential dissolution
- restore majority through coalition agreement

Partial success:

- government survives one supply vote under strict review

Failure:

- presidential refusal or forced resignation

Cleanup:

- end government-specific missions
- preserve active coalition breach evidence

### Crisis 4: presidential refusal of dissolution

Trigger:

- Taoiseach without majority requests dissolution
- another credible majority may exist

Immediate effects:

- President becomes visible actor
- government remains caretaker
- Dáil parties receive formation decisions

Objectives:

- nominate an alternative Taoiseach within 21 days
- demonstrate a majority through signed commitments

### Independents, farmer caucuses, and Clann na Talmhan

Part 2 separates formal party membership from issue-based agrarian cooperation.

#### Opening independent and farmer state

In 1936 Clann na Talmhan does not exist as a national party. Rural dissatisfaction is represented through independent deputies, county organisations, Rural Organisational Support, agricultural promises, prices, rates, roads, land access, credit, and the performance of Fianna Fáil and Fine Gael in western and export-farming constituencies.

Independent and farmer deputies can:

- support a government on confidence while opposing an agricultural bill
- demand named rural works, price guarantees, rate relief, credit, land, or local-government commitments
- join a Costello or Norton coalition under a written agrarian clause
- refuse permanent party discipline
- punish urban-first development, requisition without compensation, or attempts to absorb their organisations into a major party

#### Clann na Talmhan activation `[H]`

The party becomes available from 29 June 1939 through the working event family `Organise the Smallholders` when western agrarian organisation is viable. The historical activation recognises the Athenry founding meeting, Michael Donnellan's first leadership, differentiated rate relief, and the movement's claim to stand outside the Civil War party divide.

The lifecycle has five stages.

| Stage | Historical or plausible trigger | Active content | Exit or transformation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| western agrarian movement | date reaches 29 June 1939 and rural organisation is viable | county branches, smallholder programme, opposition to indiscriminate derating | growth, repression, absorption, or organisational failure |
| electoral test | 1940 Galway West by-election or an alternate valid by-election | candidate mission, county executive, non-party identity, first transfer analysis | foothold, respectable defeat, or collapse |
| local breakthrough | strong 1942 local performance or equivalent county objectives | control or influence in named county councils, organisers, rural legitimacy | national merger talks or regional isolation |
| united farmer party | early 1943 merger with the National Agricultural Party or a negotiated alternate compact | Michael Donnellan as leader, Patrick Cogan as deputy, minimum-price and tillage programme, formal party seat ledger | general election, regional conflict, or failed merger |
| parliamentary agrarian party | elected representation after 1943 | confidence bargaining, agrarian promise ledger, coalition access, leadership and cohesion crises | Blowick succession, faction split, coalition integration, or decline |

#### Formal seats and the Agrarian Support Bloc

The historical 1943 anchor gives Clann na Talmhan `10` formal party seats. Five additional farmer independents remain independent. The working status `Agrarian Support Bloc` may therefore coordinate as many as `15` agrarian deputies on a specific confidence, prices, rates, land, or supply question without converting those independents into party members.

The historical 1944 anchor gives Clann na Talmhan `9` formal party seats. Broader retrospective tables that group aligned farmer deputies with the party are useful for measuring agrarian influence, while the formal party and independent ledgers remain separate in this design.

`Agrarian Cohesion` is a temporary party and caucus value from `0` to `100` that appears only after Clann na Talmhan activates.

| Band | Working state | Gameplay meaning |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `0` to `24` | fractured county machines | formal party discipline is weak and the Agrarian Support Bloc cannot make joint commitments |
| `25` to `49` | regional caucuses | county executives cooperate on selected bills and remain unreliable in coalition |
| `50` to `74` | national farmer bloc | the party can negotiate confidence and a common agricultural programme |
| `75` to `100` | disciplined agrarian movement | the party can lead a rural compact while accumulating severe western and eastern faction pressure |

The value rises through county organisation, fair candidate selection, delivered agricultural promises, differentiated rate relief, local roads and credit, and respect for independent allies. It falls through urban price controls imposed without rural compensation, coercive requisition, leader patronage, failed land policy, broken coalition clauses, and attempts by Fine Gael or Fianna Fáil to turn the movement into a satellite.

#### Leadership lifecycle

- Michael Donnellan is the historical first leader and remains the default through the 1944 election.
- Patrick Cogan is deputy leader after the 1943 merger and represents the Leinster commercial-farming wing.
- Joseph Blowick becomes the historical post-1944 leadership outcome through a party conference that follows the loss of a seat and continued regional conflict.
- An alternate Donnellan continuation requires Agrarian Cohesion above `65`, a delivered coalition record, and no unresolved western versus Leinster programme crisis.
- A leader change never changes the Taoiseach automatically. Government office still requires coalition and Dáil procedures.

#### Agrarian programme and coalition behaviour

Every agrarian agreement selects three owned clauses from:

- minimum agricultural prices
- differentiated rate relief
- tillage subsidy and input access
- land redistribution or fixity of tenure
- local roads, electrification, drainage, and market access
- agricultural credit and cooperative development
- public salary restraint or administrative economy
- protection from coercive requisition

The historical 1943 behaviour is represented by a preference for an all-party national government, followed by issue-based abstention that permits the largest party to form a government while preserving opposition on agricultural questions. Alternate outcomes include formal confidence and supply, a Fine Gael-led inter-party coalition, a Norton-led social and rural compact, or a Fianna Fáil agreement with enforceable rural clauses.

#### Decision and mission content

The party and caucus unlock:

- county branch and candidate decisions with organiser, transport, and local-promise costs
- a merger-conference decision that risks western or Leinster withdrawal
- confidence and supply negotiations tied to named agrarian clauses
- price, rates, credit, and local-works missions with region-specific objectives
- a `Hold the Farmer Balance` mission during a hung Dáil
- a `Keep the County Executives Together` mission during coalition or leadership conflict
- a `Deliver the Rural Programme` mission that checks actual projects and agricultural outcomes rather than passive stockpiles

Full success creates an independent agrarian partner with delivered commitments. Partial success preserves formal seats while one regional wing becomes semi-detached. Failure produces one of three named states.

| Failure working label | Cause | Consequence | Recovery |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `The Farmer Bloc Fractures` | Agrarian Cohesion below `25`, merger breakdown, or unresolved leadership conflict | county machines act independently, coalition votes become unreliable, formal seats may defect | regional convention, revised programme, leadership settlement |
